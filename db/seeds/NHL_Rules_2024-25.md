## Hockey in 5 Minutes — A Newcomer's Quick Start

If you've never watched a hockey game, here is the entire framework on one page. Read this first; everything below is the formal rulebook that fills in the details.

**The object.** Two teams of five skaters plus a goalkeeper each try to put a vulcanized rubber **puck** into the opposing team's **net** more times than the opponent. One puck in the net = one **goal**. Most goals at the end of the game wins.

**The rink.** A rectangle of ice, 200 feet by 85 feet, with rounded corners (28-ft radius) and surrounded by waist-high **boards** (40–48", ideally 42") topped with safety glass (8' at the ends, 6' along the sides). Two **blue lines** divide the rink into thirds: each team's **defending zone** (where their own net is), the **neutral zone** in the middle, and their **attacking zone** (where the opposing net is). A **red center line** bisects the neutral zone.

**The clock.** Three **periods** of 20 minutes of *actual play* each, separated by 18-minute intermissions. The clock stops on every whistle (icing, off-side, goal, puck out of play, penalty). A 60-minute regulation game takes ~2.5 hours of real time.

**Players on the ice.** Each team carries 20 players (18 skaters + 2 goalkeepers). At full strength, 5 skaters and 1 goalkeeper are on the ice. Skaters change in **line shifts** every 30–60 seconds, often **on the fly** (without a stoppage). Positions are loosely Left Wing, Center, Right Wing (the **forward line**) and two **defensemen**. The **goalkeeper** is the only player allowed to catch or hold the puck.

**Face-offs.** Every stoppage restarts with a **face-off**: one player from each team lines up across a spot on the ice and battles for the puck the moment the official drops it. There are nine face-off spots — one at center ice, four in the neutral zone, and four in the end zones (two per end).

**The two great procedural rules.**

- **Off-side (Rule 83).** An attacker may not enter the opposing team's blue line *ahead of the puck*. The puck must enter the attacking zone first (or simultaneously with the player). Violation: whistle, face-off in the neutral zone.
- **Icing (Rule 81).** A team that is *not* short-handed cannot shoot the puck from behind its own half of the center red line across the opposing goal line untouched. Violation: whistle, face-off in the offending team's defensive zone, and the offending team **cannot change its skaters**. This punishes lazy clears. The NHL uses *hybrid icing*: the linesperson decides based on which player would reach the face-off dot first (not the puck).

**How you score.**

- The entire puck must cross the **goal line** between the posts, under the crossbar, into the net.
- A goal is **disallowed** if the puck is kicked in with a distinct kicking motion, batted in with a hand, deflected in off a high stick (above the crossbar), or scored while an attacker is interfering with the goalkeeper. Goals deflecting off an official are also disallowed.
- Most goals are reviewable. Close goals are confirmed or overturned via **video review** by the NHL Situation Room in Toronto.

**Penalties — the heart of the game.** When a player breaks a rule (hooking, tripping, slashing, etc.), the referee sends him to the **penalty box** for a set time. His team plays a man short — this is a **power play** for the other team and a **penalty kill** for the offending team.

| Penalty | Time | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Minor** | 2:00 | Standard infractions. Ends early if the opponent scores. |
| **Double-minor** | 4:00 | More serious (e.g., high stick that draws blood). |
| **Major** | 5:00 | Severe infractions or fighting. Full time served even on a goal. |
| **Misconduct** | 10:00 | Player sits 10 min; team is **not** short-handed. |
| **Game misconduct** | Ejection | Substitute allowed. Adds 10 min to player's record. |
| **Match penalty** | Ejection | Intent to injure; mandatory league review. |
| **Penalty shot** | Free shot from center | For fouls that destroyed a clear scoring chance. |

**Overtime and shootouts.**

- **Regular season:** If tied after 60 minutes, a 5-minute **3-on-3 sudden-death overtime** is played — first goal wins. If still tied, a 3-round **shootout** (then sudden-death rounds) decides it. Winner = 2 points; loser = 1.
- **Playoffs:** No shootout, ever. Tied games go to continuous **20-minute, 5-on-5 sudden-death overtime** periods until someone scores.

**Three things to listen for on the broadcast.**

- *"Delayed penalty"* — the referee has his arm up; the non-offending team can pull its goalie for an extra attacker until possession changes.
- *"Coach's challenge"* — a coach can challenge a goal for off-side, missed stoppage, or goaltender interference; or a puck-over-glass minor. Failed challenge = a delay-of-game minor (or double-minor for repeat failures).
- *"Power play"* / *"penalty kill"* — these are the two sides of the same special-teams coin.

## Section 1 — Playing Area (Rules 1–4)

### Rule 1 — Rink

*The official NHL playing surface: 200 ft × 85 ft, three zones, nine face-off spots, the trapezoid behind the net.*

- **1.1 Rink.** NHL games are played on an ice surface known as the "Rink." No ice markings are permitted except those provided for in these rules unless expressly authorized by the League. On-ice logos must not interfere with official markings. Between periods the ice surface shall be flooded unless mutually agreed otherwise.
- **1.2 Dimensions.** The official size of the rink is **two hundred feet (200') long and eighty-five feet (85') wide**. Corners are rounded in the arc of a circle with a **radius of twenty-eight feet (28')**.
- **1.3 Boards and Glass.** The rink is surrounded by a wall ("the boards") not less than **forty inches (40")** and not more than **forty-eight inches (48")** above the ice; ideal height is **42"**. The playing surface and the boards are white, except the kick plate at the bottom of the boards, which is light yellow. Approved safety glass extends **eight feet (8')** above the boards at each end and **at least six feet (6')** along the sides. Protective glass is required in front of the penalty benches.
- **1.4 Spectator Netting.** Netting is hung above the glass at the ends of the rink to protect spectators.
- **1.5 Lines.** Eleven feet (11') from each end of the rink, a 2-inch-wide red **goal line** is drawn completely across the ice and continued up the side of the boards. The ice between the goals is divided into three zones by two **blue lines**, 12 inches wide (PMS 286), drawn sixty-four feet (64') out from each goal line, parallel to the goal lines. A 12-inch-wide red **center line** (PMS 186) divides the ice exactly at center; it bears regular interval markings to distinguish it from the blue lines.
- **1.6 Division of Ice Surface.** The portion in which a team's goal sits is its **DEFENDING ZONE**; the central portion is the **NEUTRAL ZONE**; and the portion farthest from the defended goal is the **ATTACKING ZONE**.
- **1.7 Goal Crease / Referee's Crease.** The **goal crease** is laid out as follows: one foot (1') outside each goal post a 2" line extends 4'6" at right angles to the goal line, joined by a semicircle of **six foot (6') radius** centered on the middle of the goal line. On the side of the crease lines, four feet (4') from the goal line, a 5" line extends into the crease. The crease area is painted light blue (PMS 298); the area inside the goal frame to the goal line is gloss white. The crease extends vertically 4' to the top of the goal frame. The **Referee's Crease** is a 10' radius semicircle in red, 2" wide, on the ice immediately in front of the Penalty Timekeeper's seat.
- **1.8 Goalkeeper's Restricted Area (the "Trapezoid").** A trapezoid-shaped area behind each net defines where the goalkeeper may play the puck. The lines begin **six feet (6') from either goal post** and extend diagonally to points **twenty-eight feet (28') apart at the end boards**. A goalkeeper who plays the puck outside this trapezoid (and not in skate contact with his crease) is assessed a minor penalty for delay of game (see Rule 27.8).
- **1.9 Face-off Spots and Circles.** There are **nine face-off spots** total:
  - One **center ice** face-off spot (red, 12" diameter inside an X-shaped marking; circle has a 15' radius, blue).
  - **Four neutral-zone spots**, red, 2' diameter, located 5' from each blue line and 44' apart from each other across the ice.
  - **Four end-zone spots** (one on each side of each goal). End-zone spots are 2' diameter red circles, 15' radius around each. Each face-off spot has two parallel lines 3" from the top and bottom; the area within those lines is painted red, the rest white. Around the end-zone circles are hash marks (two red lines, 2" wide × 2' long, 5'7" apart). The end-zone spots are located along a line 20 feet from each goal line and 22 feet on either side of the straight line joining the centers of the two goals.
- **1.10 Ice Cleaning.** Beyond normal intermission resurfacing, snow is removed during commercial time-outs (goal crease, in front of benches, top of circles to the end boards). In the regular season at the end of the third period of a tied game, the entire ice surface is shoveled before the five-minute overtime; at the end of overtime in a tied game, it is shoveled again before the shootout.

**Rink Dimensions at a Glance**

| Element | Measurement |
|---|---|
| Rink length | 200 ft |
| Rink width | 85 ft |
| Corner radius | 28 ft |
| Boards height | 40–48" (ideal 42") |
| End glass | 8 ft above boards |
| Side glass | ≥ 6 ft above boards |
| Distance from end boards to goal line | 11 ft |
| Distance from goal line to blue line | 64 ft |
| Goal crease radius | 6 ft |
| Referee's crease radius | 10 ft |
| Face-off circle radius | 15 ft |
| Face-off spot diameter | 2 ft (1 ft at center) |
| Trapezoid: width at goal line | 22 ft (6' outside each post) |
| Trapezoid: width at end boards | 28 ft |

**In practice:** Three zones, blue lines, a red center line, nine face-off dots, and a trapezoid behind each net are the geometry every other rule references.

### Rule 2 — Goal Posts and Nets

*Standard NHL goal: 6 ft wide × 4 ft tall, secured by flexible pegs so the frame can dislodge on contact.*

- **2.1 Goal Posts.** Posts are held in position by flexible pegs (10" long, yellow) inserted in the ice or floor. The posts extend vertically **four feet (4')** above the ice and are set **six feet (6') apart measured from the inside of the posts**. A crossbar spans them. Posts and crossbar are painted red; all other exterior surfaces are white.
- **2.2 Nets.** A white nylon-cord net is attached to each goal frame and draped so a puck cannot rest outside it. A white nylon or canvas **skirt** is laced around the base plate to prevent the net from being cut. The skirt is set back six inches from the inside of the goal post and does not project more than one inch above the base plate. The rear/top mesh is heat-set, resin-treated twine (5 mm diameter or equivalent multifilament white nylon, 550–600 lb break strength), with 2½" mesh, laced to the frame with No. 21 white nylon cord. Thin top netting is 12-ply, four-braid, 62 mm inside-to-inside mesh.

**In practice:** Pegs are flexible by design — when a player crashes into the net the frame pops off rather than injures, but the game stops because the goal is "displaced."

### Rule 3 — Benches

*Each rink has matching players' benches and two separate penalty benches across the ice.*

- **3.1 Players' Benches.** Each rink has uniform players' benches for both teams seating at least **14 persons per team**, immediately alongside the ice as near to center as possible, ideally **24 feet long**. Each bench has two uniform doors. All doors open *inward* to the playing surface. Benches are mirrored in location and size.
- **3.2 Penalty Bench.** Each rink has a "**Penalty Bench**" capable of seating ten persons including off-ice officials. Separate penalty benches are provided for each team on opposite sides of the Timekeeper's area, **directly across from the players' benches** and situated in the neutral zone. Each penalty bench is separated from spectators by glass at least 5 feet above the boards.

**In practice:** The penalty benches must be in the neutral zone so a penalized player exits onto neutral ice, not into either team's defensive end.

### Rule 4 — Signal and Timing Devices

*The horn, the lights, and the clocks that drive the entire game.*

- **4.1 Signal Devices.** Each rink must have an automatic sound device that sounds at the end of each period. If it fails, the **game clock is the determining factor**, not the horn. Behind each goal, a **red light** signifies a goal scored; a **green light** signifies the end of a period or game. *A goal cannot be scored when the green light is showing.* A red light at or near the timekeeper's bench is illuminated during commercial time-outs.
- **4.2 Timing Devices.** Each rink has an electronic clock showing time remaining in the period and time remaining for at least five penalized players per team. The game clock shows tenths of a second during the final minute of each period.

**In practice:** If you ever see a puck cross the line *just* as the horn sounds, the controlling question is whether the green light came on first — not the horn.

## Section 2 — Teams (Rules 5–8)

### Rule 5 — Team

*A team carries 20 players (18 skaters + 2 goalkeepers). The lineup must be submitted before the game and cannot be changed afterwards.*

- **5.1 Eligible Players.** A team is composed of **twenty (20) players (18 skaters and 2 goalkeepers)** under contract to the Club. The visiting team's Manager or Coach submits a list of eligible players via the NHL Game Line-up Management application within **five minutes of the completion of warm-up** (20 minutes before face-off). Once submitted, no changes are permitted after the commencement of the game. If an official notices before the game that an in-uniform player was not listed, the Referee notifies the offending team so the lineup can be corrected with no penalty.
- **5.2 Ineligible Player.** Only players on the submitted lineup may play. The determining factor is the player's **name**, not the number. An ineligible player is removed from the game; no substitution is allowed. The Club may not be able to substitute another player. A goal scored while an ineligible player is on the ice is disallowed (only the goal scored at that stoppage; previous goals stand). The matter is reported to the Commissioner.
- **5.3 Goalkeeper.** Each team has **one goalkeeper on the ice and a substitute goalkeeper fully equipped and ready** on or near the bench. No skater may wear goalie equipment unless both listed goalkeepers are incapacitated. If both are incapacitated in a regular-season or playoff game, the team may dress and play any eligible goalkeeper. A third goalkeeper recalled because both regulars were injured in quick succession is given a reasonable time to dress plus a two-minute warm-up (none if he is already on the bench when the second goalie goes down, and none if he is entering to defend a penalty shot). Recalls of minor league goalkeepers after suspensions under Rule 11 count as emergency recalls within the 23-man roster limit.
- **5.4 Coaches and Team Personnel.** Only players in uniform and registered non-playing personnel (Manager, Coach(es), Trainer, Equipment Manager, etc.) may occupy the benches.

**In practice:** The "names not numbers" rule prevents teams from sneaking an unlisted player into a game by giving him a listed teammate's number.

### Rule 6 — Captain and Alternate Captains

*One Captain (or none), up to three Alternates. Only the on-ice Captain may discuss rule interpretations with the Referee.*

- **6.1 Captain.** Each team appoints **one Captain**, who alone may discuss rule interpretations with the Referee. He wears a **"C" approximately three inches (3") in height** in contrasting color on the front of his sweater. **No co-Captains.** A team may have one Captain plus up to two Alternates, or no Captain plus up to three Alternates. Only the Captain — when invited by the Referee — may discuss rule interpretation. Any Captain, Alternate Captain or player coming off the bench to protest a call is assessed a minor for unsportsmanlike conduct (Rule 39 — Abuse of Officials); if it continues, a misconduct, then a game misconduct. A complaint about a penalty is **not** a "matter relating to the interpretation of the rules."
- **6.2 Alternate Captains.** When the Captain is not on the ice, an Alternate on the ice has Captain privileges. Alternates wear an **"A" approximately three inches (3") in height** in contrasting color. Only when the Captain is not in uniform (or none exists) may the Coach designate three Alternates, before the game starts. No playing Coach, playing Manager, or goalkeeper may serve as Captain or Alternate.

**In practice:** When players swarm a ref after a controversial call, only the player wearing the C (or, in his absence, an A) has standing to actually ask questions.

### Rule 7 — Starting Line-up

*Visiting team names its five starters first; home team responds. Once approved, the starting five cannot change without referee approval.*

- **7.1 Starting Line-up.** With the lineup submission, the **visiting team's Manager or Coach names the starting line-up**. The home team's Manager or Coach, knowing the visitor's five, then names his starting line-up. No change to either starting line-up may be made unless reviewed and approved by the Referee prior to the start of the game.
- **7.2 Violation.** For an infraction, a **bench minor** is imposed. This is an appeal play and must be raised before the second face-off. No penalty if the appeal is not sustained. The determining factor is the **name**, not the number. If a team scores on the first shift and it is then brought to the Referee's attention that the scoring team had an incorrect starting line-up, the goal is allowed but a bench minor is assessed. If the scoring team correctly challenges the opposing starting line-up on the same shift in which they just scored, the goal nullifies the bench minor and no further penalties are assessed.

**In practice:** This is essentially a procedural rule — virtually never invoked in modern hockey because lineups are submitted electronically.

### Rule 8 — Injured Players

*An injured player can be replaced; play continues unless the injury is serious. Bleeding players must leave the ice until cleaned up.*

- **8.1 Injured Player.** A player injured during the game may retire and be replaced by a substitute; play continues without the teams leaving the ice. During play an injured player wishing to retire must do so **at the players' bench, not any other exit** (otherwise it is an illegal change — bench minor). A penalized but injured player may go to the dressing room; the penalized team must place a substitute on the penalty bench (for a minor, immediately if play is stopped or the team chooses, and for a major, before the penalty expires). The injured player must wait until his substitute has been released from the penalty box before he is eligible to return.
- When a player is injured so badly he cannot continue or reach his bench, **play is not stopped until the injured player's team has secured control of the puck**. If the injured player's team is in control, play is stopped immediately unless they are in a scoring position. The Referee or Linesperson may stop play immediately for an obvious serious injury.
- **Face-off following injury stoppage:** The ensuing face-off is conducted at the face-off spot in the zone nearest the puck's location when play was stopped — unless the injured player's team controlled the puck in the attacking zone (then face-off is in the neutral zone), or the injured player is in his defending zone and the opposing team controls the puck in the attacking zone (then face-off is at one of the defending team's end-zone spots).
- A player attended to on the ice by Trainer/medical personnel must be substituted for immediately and cannot return until play resumes.
- **8.2 Injured Goalkeeper.** A goalkeeper sustaining an injury must be ready to resume play immediately or be replaced by a substitute; no additional time is allowed for the injured goalie to resume his position. A replacement is allowed a two-minute warm-up in pre-season only, **no warm-up in regular-season or playoff games**. When a goalkeeper is removed for mandatory medical evaluation, the replacement may have a brief warm-up. Any delay in making the substitution is reported to the Commissioner. If the trainer comes onto the ice and there is no undue delay, the goalkeeper may stay in the game with no warm-up.
- **8.3 Blood.** Any player bleeding or with visible blood on his equipment or body **shall be ruled off the ice at the next stoppage** and may not return until bleeding has stopped and the cut is covered (if necessary). Any affected equipment/uniform must be properly decontaminated or exchanged.

**In practice:** Hockey's "play continues until possession changes" injury rule is unusual — it exists because stopping play immediately would too often hand a scoring chance to the wrong team.

## Section 3 — Equipment (Rules 9–14)

### Rule 9 — Uniforms

*Helmets are mandatory; modified/altered uniforms are not permitted. Players coming off after their helmet falls off in play face a minor.*

- **9.1 Team Uniform.** Players are dressed uniformly with approved design and color of helmets, sweaters, short pants, stockings and skates. **Altered uniforms (Velcro inserts, oversized jerseys, altered collars) are not permitted.** Home and road uniforms must be distinctive and contrasting; no parts are interchangeable except the pants.
- **9.2 Numbers.** Each player wears an identifying number **at least ten inches (10") high** on the back. **Numbers like 00, ½, .05, 101 are not permitted.** Surnames appear in full in block letters **3" high** across the back at shoulder height.
- **9.3 Player's Jersey.** Maximum size per manufacturer template. **No inserts or additions** are allowed. No alterations to the neck opening. Sleeves must extend into the glove cuff. Jerseys must be properly **"tied down"** at all times.
- **9.4 Goalkeeper's Jersey.** Standard goalkeeper-cut, no inserts/additions. No "tying down" at the wrists that creates a "webbing effect" in the armpit. The jersey is illegal if it covers any area between the goalkeeper's legs.
- **9.5 Protective Equipment.** All protective equipment except **gloves, headgear, and goalkeepers' leg pads** must be worn under the uniform. After warning by the Referee, a second violation is a minor. A player refusing to change/remove non-conforming equipment is assessed a minor for delay of game; if he returns to the ice without making the change, a misconduct; a third time, a game misconduct. Pants must be one color around and throughout the leg, not ripped/cut/torn.
- **9.6 Helmets.** All players must wear a League-approved helmet. **Players entering the NHL beginning with the 2019-2020 season** must also wear a helmet during pre-game warm-up. A player whose helmet comes off during play is assessed a **minor** if he does not exit the ice or retrieve and replace the helmet (with or without strap fastened) within a reasonable period — though he may complete a play if he is making one or in the vicinity of the puck. A player returning to the ice from his players' bench during play, or from the penalty bench during play, must have his helmet on with chin strap properly fastened (or a minor results). When a goalkeeper loses his helmet/mask: play is stopped immediately if his team has possession; otherwise stopped only if there is no immediate scoring opportunity. Ensuing face-off is in the defending team's end zone. A goalkeeper who **deliberately removes** his helmet/mask to gain a stoppage receives a minor for delay of game. If a goalkeeper deliberately removes his helmet/mask while the opposing team is on a breakaway, or during a penalty shot/shootout attempt, **a goal is awarded**. A player who intentionally removes an opponent's helmet during play is assessed a minor for roughing (Rule 51).
- **9.7 Visors.** Starting with the 2013–2014 season, all players with **fewer than 25 games of NHL experience** must wear a visor properly affixed.
- **9.8 Dangerous Equipment.** Pads/protectors of metal or any material likely to cause injury are prohibited. Referees may prohibit any equipment they feel may injure. Failure to comply = minor for delay of game. An approved face mask/protector may be worn by a player with a facial injury. A modified stick whose edges have not been beveled is dangerous equipment and is removed from the game; no penalty unless the player returns with it unmodified.

**In practice:** The visor rule is grandfathered — most veterans without one entered the league before 2013. The "helmet comes off in play" rule is why you see players stop and pick theirs up rather than make one more play without it.

### Rule 10 — Sticks

*Strict dimensions on shaft length, blade length/width and curvature. Illegal-stick penalties escalate fast.*

- **10.1 Player's Stick.** The blade is not more than **three inches (3") in width** at any point between the heel and ½" in from the mid-point of the tip, and not less than two inches (2"). All edges of the blade are beveled. The **curvature** of the blade is restricted so the perpendicular distance from a straight line from the heel to the end of the blade to the point of maximum curvature does not exceed **¾ inch**. **No stick exceeds 63 inches** in length from heel to end of shaft, nor 12½ inches from heel to end of blade. Length-of-shaft exceptions may be granted on application.
- **10.2 Goalkeeper's Stick.** A goalkeeper's stick has a knob of white tape (or approved protective material) **not less than ½" thick** at the top of the shaft. Failure to comply makes the stick unfit; it must be changed without a minor penalty. Blade may not exceed **3½" in width** at any point except at the heel (max **4½"**); blade may not exceed **15½" in length** from heel to end. **No measurement of any goalkeeper's stick during the course of a game.** The widened "paddle" extending up the shaft from the blade may not extend more than **26"** from the heel nor exceed **3½" in width**. Paddle-length exemptions: only for players **6'6" or taller**, up to a max paddle of 26".
- **10.3 Broken Stick — Player.** A broken stick is one which, in the Referee's opinion, is unfit for normal play. **A player without a stick may participate**; a player whose stick is broken may participate only if he drops the broken stick (a minor penalty otherwise). A replacement may come from his own bench, from a teammate on the ice, or by picking up his own or a teammate's unbroken stick from the ice. **Throwing or shooting a stick to a teammate is a minor**; picking up an opponent's stick or playing with a goalkeeper's stick is a minor. The player using a stick thrown from the bench is not penalized, but the person who threw it gets a bench minor.
- **10.4 Broken Stick — Goalkeeper.** A goalkeeper may continue with a broken stick until a stoppage or until a teammate legally hands him one (teammate may not throw or shoot it to him — minor for the teammate, no penalty to the goalie). A goalkeeper with a broken/illegal stick may **not** go to the bench for a replacement; he must receive it from a teammate. He may use a skater's stick until legally replaced.
- **10.5 Stick Measurement.** Limited to one request per team per stoppage. Stick measurement is conducted at the penalty bench: the Referee draws an imaginary line along the outside of the shaft to the bottom of the blade to determine the heel, then measures curvature with a League-approved gauge from heel to any point along the toe. Other dimensions are measured by tape. **Result of an illegal stick:** minor penalty + **$200 fine** for the player. Second offense in the same season: same minor + **$1,000 fine**. Third offense: minor + **game misconduct + automatic one-game suspension**. Further violations in the same season: suspension doubles each time. **Unsustained complaint:** bench minor for the complaining team. Deliberately breaking a stick or refusing to surrender it for measurement: **minor + 10-minute misconduct + $200 fine**.
- **10.6 Stick Measurement — Prior to Penalty Shot.** *First stick:* if legal, complaining club gets a bench minor (regardless of shot result). If illegal, the player is assessed a minor; he must obtain a new (second) stick before the shot and serve his penalty after. *Second stick:* told the second stick will be measured first; if legal, proceed; if illegal, disallow the opportunity and assess one minor for the first illegal stick. Refusing to surrender for measurement: shot is disallowed and player gets a misconduct.
- **10.7 Stick Measurement — Prior to Shootout Attempt.** If the stick is **legal**, the complaining club **forfeits its next shootout attempt** + $1,000 fine to Coach + $5,000 fine to Club. If illegal, the offending team forfeits the attempt and the player becomes ineligible for the shootout + $1,000 fine to player + $5,000 to Club. Shootout stick measurements are not subject to the fines/suspensions of 10.5.
- **10.8 Illegal Stick.** Following a complaint and inspection, an illegal stick is one that does not conform to the rule.

**Stick Dimensions Summary**

| Element | Player's Stick | Goalkeeper's Stick |
|---|---|---|
| Max shaft length | 63" (heel to end of shaft) | — |
| Max blade length | 12½" (heel to end of blade) | 15½" |
| Max blade width | 3" | 3½" (4½" at heel) |
| Min blade width | 2" | — |
| Max curvature | ¾" | — |
| Paddle width (goalie) | n/a | 3½", up to 26" from heel |

**In practice:** Illegal-stick penalties are rare but famously decisive — a coach who calls measurement at the wrong time gives the opposition a free power play.

### Rule 11 — Goalkeeper's Equipment

*All goalie equipment must be solely for protection — no "stopping-area inflation." League sizes equipment to each goalie's body.*

- **11.1 Goalkeeper's Equipment.** Except for skates and stick, all goalie equipment must be **constructed solely for the purpose of protection**. He may not wear any garment or use any contrivance that gives him "undue assistance in keeping goal." The Hockey Operations Department may inspect equipment at any time.
- **11.2 Leg Pads.** Maximum width **11 inches** at the leg. Pads must be **anatomically proportional and size-specific** to the individual. The Hockey Operations Department assigns each goalkeeper a **Limiting Distance Size** — sum of floor-to-knee and 45% of knee-to-pelvis measurements plus a 4" allowance for skate height — measured vertically from the ice. Pads exceeding the Limiting Distance Size are illegal for that goalkeeper. Minimum **boot length 7"**. Pad depth max **10½"** (front face to last edge of inner knee protection). Defined boot channel required. **Calf-wing protectors** must be inset ½" from the inside 5-hole, one piece, no wedges, max 1" thick, attached to the back cover with straps that wrap; no zippers, Velcro, or Velcro attachments. Calf-wings may be flat for 5½" before contouring. **Knee strap pad** max 6" long × 2½" thick (non-compressed); knee straps must wrap around the knee build-ups (not behind or through). Medial rolls are not permitted. All knee protection must be worn under the thigh guard of the pant.
- **11.3 Chest and Arm Pads.** Must be anatomically proportional and size-specific. No raised ridges on front edges/sides/inside/outside of arms/across shoulders. Lateral edge layering permitted for rib protection, not to exceed thickness of front blocks; flank protection must wrap around the torso.
  - **Elbow floaters:** Max 6" wide across the front; max 7" depth; total length max 6½" from proximal end of biceps segment to distal end of forearm segment. Symmetrically positioned over the elbow.
  - **Bicep pad:** Upper portion max **5½"**, lower portion max **4½"**.
  - **Forearm pad:** Upper portion max **4½"**, lower portion max **4"**.
  - **Clavicle floaters:** Must not extend laterally beyond the player's shoulder or the player's torso below the chest, nor beyond the armpit at the level of the axilla. Vertical projection above the shoulder: ≤ 2" at lateral edge, ≤ 1½" at midpoint of its width, ≤ ½" at medial edge.
  - **Shoulder caps:** Must follow the shoulder contour; lateral projection ≤ 1½".
  - The unit may not ride upward or remain above the shoulder when the goalie drops to a butterfly or kneeling position. **No additions, straps, laces, attachments after inspection.** League sets each goalkeeper's max based on shoulder width, torso width/length, arm length.
- **11.4 Pants.** Anatomically proportional and size-specific. Each goalie is assigned a pant size. Waist max **6" tall** as measured from where it meets the body of the pant. **Inner circumference of thigh/leg opening max 32"**. No internal/external padding beyond protective; no lacing/rib knit/belts/eyelets that allow waist circumference increase. **Thigh guard** measured 5" up from bottom of pant while standing; groin/hip pads cannot extend beyond the front thigh pad and are part of the 9" thigh guard measurement. Each thigh guard:
  - Outer width ≤ **9"**, with curvature depth between **4½" and 5½"**;
  - Inner arc length **14–16"**;
  - Length **8–11"**;
  - Thickness ≤ **1⅜"** at widest;
  - Inside/outside thigh cradle padding ≤ 1", attached inside the curved thigh guard, not creating a seam/ridge or extending beyond the curvature.
- **11.5 Knee Pads.** All knee protection strapped and fit under the thigh pad of the pant leg; max contoured **9"**, no flat surfaces; not permanently attached to the pants. Layering at the knee permitted for protection but not for stopping area.
- **11.6 Catching Glove.** Maximum **perimeter 45"** (distance around the glove). Wrist cuff **4" in width** (the portion protecting the wrist from where the thumb joint meets the wrist). Cuff max **8" in length**. Distance from heel along the pocket following the contour of the inside of the trap to the top of the "T" trap may not exceed **18"**. The heel is where the straight vertical line from the cuff meets the glove.
- **11.7 Blocking Glove.** Protective padding on the back of the blocking glove **does not exceed 8" wide nor 15" long** at any point (including bindings, measured along the contour of the back). Must be rectangular. Thumb/wrist flap fastened, follows the contour of the thumb and wrist; thumb protection **max 7"** in length from the top of the blocking surface. No raised ridges. All goalkeepers must use one blocking glove and one catching glove meeting League-approved sizing.
- **11.8 Masks.** Protective masks of a League-approved design must be worn. Masks deemed to be worn only to increase stopping area are illegal.
- **11.9 League Inspections.** Inspections may take place any time before, during, or after any game. Any violation = **automatic two-game suspension** to the offending goalkeeper. If both goalkeepers on a Club have illegal equipment, both are suspended in sequence (the goalie who played that day suspended for the next two; the back-up for the subsequent two). A goalkeeper playing with uninspected equipment, or tampering with inspected equipment, is suspended two games + **Club fined $25,000 + Equipment Manager fined $1,000**. Each additional violation: all suspensions/fines double. Refusal to submit equipment: same sanctions.

**In practice:** The league now sizes goalie equipment to the body of each individual goalie, so a 5'9" goalie cannot wear the same maxed-out pads as a 6'5" goalie.

### Rule 12 — Illegal Equipment

*Catch-all for non-conforming protective gear and gloves with cut palms.*

- **12.1 Illegal Equipment.** All protective equipment except gloves, headgear and goalkeepers' leg pads must be worn under the uniform. After warning, a minor is imposed. Players violating may not participate until corrected/removed.
- **12.2 Gloves.** A glove from which all or part of the palm has been removed or cut to permit bare-hand use is illegal — minor on the player. An unsustained complaint of this nature is a bench minor for delaying the game against the complaining club.
- **12.3 Elbow Pads.** Elbow pads without a soft protective outer covering of sponge rubber or similar material **at least ½" thick** are considered dangerous equipment.
- **12.4 Fair Play.** These equipment regulations are written in the spirit of fair play; if the spirit is abused, the offending equipment is ineligible until a hearing.
- **12.5 League Inspections.** Hockey Operations, Officiating and/or Security may inspect at any time.

**In practice:** Cut-palm gloves were common decades ago for stick-handling feel; they are flatly banned today.

### Rule 13 — Puck

*The standard NHL puck: 1" thick, 3" diameter, 5½–6 oz., frozen before use.*

- **13.1 Dimensions.** Vulcanized rubber (or approved material), **1" thick**, **3" in diameter**, weighing between **5½ and 6 ounces**. All pucks must be League-approved.
- **13.2 Supply.** The home team provides an adequate supply, **kept frozen**, at the penalty bench under the control of an Off-Ice Official or attendant.
- **13.3 Illegal Puck.** If a second puck appears on the ice during play, play is **not stopped**; play continues with the legal puck until the play in progress is completed by change of possession.

**In practice:** Pucks are frozen to keep them from bouncing — a warm puck is hard to handle and "flutters" on shots.

### Rule 14 — Adjustment to Clothing or Equipment

*Play does not stop for adjustments; players must leave the ice to fix gear.*

- **14.1 Adjustment to Clothing or Equipment.** Play is not stopped nor the game delayed for adjustments. The onus is on the player; if adjustments are needed he must leave the ice and a substitute takes his place. **No delay is permitted for goalkeeper's equipment repair/adjustment** — the goalie leaves the ice and the substitute goalie comes in immediately.

**In practice:** This is why a goalie with a broken strap during play has to skate to the bench rather than stop the game.

## Section 4 — Types of Penalties (Rules 15–29)

### Rule 15 — Calling of Penalties

*Penalty against the side WITH the puck = whistle immediately. Penalty against the side WITHOUT the puck = delayed (raised arm) until they regain possession.*

- **15.1 Calling a Penalty.** A penalty against the team **in control of the puck** = whistle blown immediately. A penalty against the team **not in control of the puck** = Referee raises his arm to signal a **delayed penalty**; when the offending team gains control, the whistle is blown. Players, Trainers, Managers, Coaches or non-playing Club personnel ejected from the game must vacate the players' bench area and may not further participate (including via radio).
- **15.2 Calling a Minor Penalty — Goal Scored.** If only a minor would be assessed and a goal is scored on the play by the non-offending side, **the minor is not imposed**. Majors and matches are imposed regardless. With two or more signaled minors and a goal scored, the Captain designates which one is washed out.
- **15.3 Calling a Double-minor — Goal Scored.** For double-minors under Rule 47 (Head-butting), Rule 58 (Butt-ending), Rule 60 (High-sticking) or Rule 62 (Spearing), if a goal is scored on the delayed call, **only 2 minutes are assessed**. Announced as a double-minor; player serves 2:00.
- **15.4 Calling a Penalty — Short-handed Team — Goal Scored.** If a team is short-handed and a *further* minor/bench minor is signaled against them, and a goal is scored before the whistle, **the goal is allowed and the further penalty is assessed; the first of the minors already being served terminates** (Rule 16). Majors/matches are imposed regardless. If short-handed only by reason of a major/match and a goal is scored before play can be stopped for a further minor/bench minor, the signaled minor/bench minor **is not imposed** due to the goal.
- **15.5 Face-off Locations.** When a penalty puts time on the clock, the ensuing face-off is at one of the two face-off spots in the **offending team's end zone**, with **three exceptions**:
  - (i) penalty after a goal — face-off at center ice;
  - (ii) penalty at the end or start of a period — face-off at center ice;
  - (iii) defending team penalized and attacking players entered the attacking zone beyond the outer edge of the end-zone face-off circle — face-off in the neutral zone (see Rule 76.2, paragraph 10).

**In practice:** The "delayed penalty + pull the goalie" play hinges on Rule 15.1: until the offending team touches the puck, the non-offending team can attack 6-on-5.

### Rule 16 — Minor Penalties

*Two minutes, no substitute. Minor ends if the opponent scores.*

- **16.1 Minor Penalty.** Any player other than a goalkeeper is ruled off the ice for **two (2) minutes**; **no substitute is permitted**.
- **16.2 Short-handed.** "Short-handed" = team is below the numerical strength of its opponent on the ice when the goal is scored. The minor/bench minor that **terminates automatically** is the one with the **least amount of time on the clock**. **Coincident minors to both teams do not cause either side to be short-handed** (see Rule 19). If short-handed by one or more minors/bench minors and the opposing team scores, the **first** such penalty terminates automatically. This applies when a goal is awarded — but **no penalty expires when a goal is scored on a penalty shot**. When two minor penalties of the same team end simultaneously, the Captain designates which player returns first.
- **16.3 Infractions.** See Reference Tables — Table 2: Summary of Minor Penalties.

**In practice:** This is why power-play goals visibly "release" a player from the box even though clock time remains.

### Rule 17 — Bench Minor Penalties

*Two minutes served by a designated teammate; same effect as a minor.*

- **17.1 Bench Minor Penalty.** One player (designated by the Coach through the Captain) is removed from the ice for **two (2) minutes** and serves the penalty as if it were a minor on him. Goalkeepers cannot serve a bench minor.
- **17.2 Short-handed.** See Rule 16.2.
- **17.3 Infractions.** See Reference Tables — Table 3.

**In practice:** "Too many men on the ice" (Rule 74) is the most common bench minor and the easiest one to spot live.

### Rule 18 — Double-minor Penalties

*Four minutes, no substitute. If a goal is scored during the first half, the second 2:00 continues.*

- **18.1 Double-minor Penalty.** Any player other than a goalkeeper is ruled off for **four (4) minutes**; **no substitute**.
- **18.2 Short-handed.** See Rule 16.2. When a double-minor is signaled and the non-offending team scores during the delay, **one of the minors is washed out** and the player serves only the remaining 2:00. Announced as a double-minor with 2:00 on the clock.
- **18.3 Infractions.** See Reference Tables — Table 4 (Butt-ending, Coach's-Challenge delay-of-game, Head-butting, High-sticking, Spearing).

**In practice:** Double-minors almost always come from high-sticks that draw blood.

### Rule 19 — Coincidental Penalties

*Equal-and-opposite penalties wash out — teams play 4-on-4 if one minor each at the same stoppage with no other penalties in effect.*

- **19.1 Coincidental Minor Penalties.** Equal-duration coincidental minors: penalized players serve their time and do not leave until the first stoppage **after their respective penalties expire**. **Immediate substitution** for the equal number of minors; these penalties are not counted for the delayed-penalty rule (Rule 26). *This applies only when at least one team is already serving a time penalty.* When **one minor is assessed to one player of each team** at the same stoppage with no other penalties in effect, these penalties are **served without substitution**, and **both teams play 4-on-4** for the duration. (If either also receives a misconduct, the team must still place a player on the penalty bench to serve the minor; teams still play 4-on-4 for the minor's duration.) For coincidentals that carry over into or are assessed during regular-season overtime, see Rule 84.3 and Tables 17–18.
- **19.2 Coincidental Major Penalties.** Equal-duration coincidental majors (incl. major + match): players serve their time, do not leave until the first stoppage after expiry. Immediate substitution for the equal number; these are not counted for the delayed-penalty rule. If one or both players also received a game misconduct in addition to their major, no substitutes are required on the penalty bench.
- **19.3 Coincidental Match Penalties.** Match-penalty players are removed from the game. Remaining penalized players stay on the bench until the first stoppage after expiry. Immediate substitution for the equal number; not counted for the delayed-penalty rule.
- **19.4 Last Five Minutes and Overtime.** During the **last 5 minutes of regulation or any time in overtime**, when a minor (or double-minor) is assessed to one player of Team A and a major (or match) to one player of Team B at the same stoppage, the **3-minute (or 1-minute) differential is served immediately as a major penalty**. Applies regardless of on-ice strength when the penalties are assessed.
- **19.5 Applying the Coincidental Penalty Rule.** When multiple penalties are assessed to both teams at the same stoppage, Referees:
  - (i) Cancel as many majors and/or match penalties as possible;
  - (ii) Cancel as many minor, bench minor, and double-minor penalties as possible;
  - (iii) Any differential is served in normal manner and displayed on the penalty clock.

**In practice:** Coincidentals are why scuffles after the whistle (each side gets a roughing minor) result in 4-on-4 play — and 4-on-4 creates more open ice and often more goals.

### Rule 20 — Major Penalties

*Five minutes, no substitute, **full time served** even on goals.*

- **20.1 Major Penalty.** For the first major in any one game, the offender (except the goalkeeper) is ruled off for **five (5) minutes**; no substitute is permitted. When one player receives a major and a minor at the same time, the major is served first (except under Rule 19.2 where coincidental majors are in effect, in which case the minor is recorded and served first).
- **20.2 Short-handed.** A major **does** cause short-handedness, but the penalized player **does not leave the box when the opposing team scores**. He must wait for the entire major to expire.
- **20.3 Substitution.** When a player has been assessed a major and removed from the game (or is injured), the team need not place a substitute on the penalty bench immediately — but **must do so at a stoppage before the major expires**. If the major is combined with assessed minors that must run on the clock, a substitute is required immediately. Failure to place a player results in the team continuing one player short (but not officially short-handed) until the next stoppage; any replacement entering other than from the penalty bench is illegal substitution (Rule 68) = bench minor. Furthermore, the team is not permitted to ice the puck since they are no longer short-handed by a penalty.
- **20.4 Automatic Game Misconduct.** Applied to any player assessed a **third major in the same game**. Also automatic for the majors listed in Tables 6 and 7 (the latter requires injury to face or head).
- **20.5 Infractions.** See Table 5.
- **20.6 On-Ice Video Review of Major Penalties.** Referees **shall review** all plays resulting in any Major Penalty (other than for fighting) for the purpose of confirming or modifying the original call. Reviews are conducted exclusively by the Referee(s) on the ice in consultation with other On-Ice Officials using technology (tablet/monitor) per Rule 38.5. The former Referee and Game Logger in the Situation Room assist with replays only; no other contact with non-game participants. Options after review:
  - (i) Confirm the original major;
  - (ii) Reduce to a lesser penalty;
  - (iii) Rescind the major altogether.

**In practice:** Majors don't expire on goals — a major plus a power-play goal still means 4 more minutes of short-handed play.

### Rule 21 — Match Penalties

*Ejection from the game + a 5-minute team penalty + automatic league review. Reserved for deliberate attempts to injure.*

- **21.1 Match Penalty.** Player is suspended for the balance of the game and ordered to the dressing room immediately. A match penalty is imposed on any player who **deliberately attempts to injure or who deliberately injures** an opponent in any manner.
- **21.2 Short-handed.** A substitute player is permitted to replace the penalized player **after five (5) minutes** of playing time has elapsed.
- **21.3 Records.** For all match penalties, a total of **15 minutes** is charged in the records (5 minutes on the clock + 10 minutes for being removed from the game). The player is automatically suspended pending review by the Commissioner.
- **21.4 Infractions.** See Table 8 (match penalty must be assessed when injury results in specific categories).
- **21.5 On-Ice Video Review of Match Penalties.** Referees shall review all match-penalty plays to confirm or modify. Options: confirm, reduce, rescind.

**In practice:** Match penalties typically trigger a hearing with the Department of Player Safety and a multi-game suspension.

### Rule 22 — Misconduct Penalties

*10 minutes in the box but team plays at full strength (a substitute takes the player's spot).*

- **22.1 Misconduct Penalty.** Player (except goalkeeper) is ruled off the ice for **10 minutes**. **A substitute is permitted** to immediately replace the player. The player whose misconduct expired remains in the box until the next stoppage.
- **22.2 Misconduct Penalty — Goalkeeper.** Served by another teammate on the ice when play was stopped, designated by the Coach through the Captain.
- **22.3 Short-handed.** A misconduct alone does **not** cause short-handedness unless combined with a minor/major/match. With a minor + misconduct: the team places a substitute on the bench to serve the minor without change; if the opponent scores during the minor, the minor terminates and the misconduct begins immediately.
- **22.4 Reporting.** All misconducts for abuse of officials are reported in detail to the League office.
- **22.5 Infractions.** See Table 9.

**In practice:** Misconducts are most often for verbal abuse of officials or for continuing/inciting a scrum after a whistle.

### Rule 23 — Game Misconduct Penalties

*Ejection; substitute allowed. Accumulates against the player and triggers automatic suspensions.*

- **23.1 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Suspension for the balance of the game; a substitute is permitted to replace the player. **10 minutes** are applied to the player's record.
- **23.2 Fines and Suspensions.** Reported to the Commissioner who may impose further fines/suspensions. A game misconduct that already triggered an automatic suspension or supplementary discipline does not count toward category accumulations. Auto-suspensions under this section do not affect playoff games. Any request to review/rescind a game misconduct must be made in writing within **48 hours**; otherwise upheld.
- **23.3 Fines and Suspensions — General Category.** **Three game misconducts** in regular-season "General Category" = automatic one-game suspension; each subsequent = +1 game. Club fined $1,000 per suspension. Playoffs: **two** game misconducts = automatic suspension; each subsequent +1 game; Club $1,000 per suspension. General Category includes:

  | (i) Fighting off the playing surface | Rule 46 |
  | --- | --- |
  | (ii) Inciting an opponent into incurring a penalty | Rule 75 |
  | (iii) Intervening in an altercation | Rule 46 |
  | (iv) Not properly tied down during an altercation | Rule 46 |
  | (v) Obscene language or gestures | Rule 75 |
  | (vi) Persists in continuing an altercation | Rule 46 |
  | (vii) Removing jersey prior to an altercation | Rule 46 |
  | (viii) Secondary altercation | Rule 46 |
  | (ix) Third major penalty in a game | Rule 20 |
  | (x) Throwing stick outside playing area | Rule 53 |

- **23.4 Fines and Suspensions — Abuse of Officials Category.** **Two** game misconducts under Rule 39 = automatic next-game suspension; each subsequent +1 game.
- **23.5 Fines and Suspensions — Stick Infractions Category.** Two regular-season game misconducts for **stick-related infractions** (before playing 41 consecutive regular-season games without one) = automatic next-game suspension; each subsequent +1. In playoffs: two stick-infraction game misconducts = automatic playoff-game suspension; each subsequent +1. Reset before each Stanley Cup Final. Stick Infractions Category:

  | (i) Butt-ending | Rule 58 |
  | --- | --- |
  | (ii) Cross-checking | Rule 59 |
  | (iii) Hooking | Rule 55 |
  | (iv) Slashing | Rule 61 |
  | (v) Spearing | Rule 62 |

- **23.6 Fines and Suspensions — Physical Infractions Category.** Two regular-season game misconducts in "Physical Infractions" (before 41 consecutive regular-season games without one) = automatic suspension; each subsequent +1. Playoffs: two = automatic suspension; +1 each subsequent. Reset before each Stanley Cup Final. Physical Infractions Category:

  | (i) Boarding | Rule 41 |
  | --- | --- |
  | (ii) Charging | Rule 42 |
  | (iii) Checking from Behind | Rule 43 |
  | (iv) Clipping | Rule 44 |
  | (v) Elbowing | Rule 45 |
  | (vi) Head-butting | Rule 47 |
  | (vii) Interference | Rule 56 |
  | (viii) Kneeing | Rule 50 |

- **23.7** Player's total games played covers a **two-year period** from the date of the first game misconduct in that category.
- **23.8 Other Infractions That Could Result in a Game Misconduct.** See Table 10. Also: interfering with or striking a spectator; racial taunts or slurs; spitting on/at an opponent or spectator. Any player or non-playing personnel who physically interferes with spectators automatically incurs a game misconduct.

**In practice:** The 41-game "clean" reset means a player who fights infrequently can usually avoid the accumulation suspensions.

### Rule 24 — Penalty Shot

*A free shot from center ice, awarded when a foul destroys a clear scoring chance.*

- **24.1 Penalty Shot.** Designed to **restore a scoring opportunity lost** as the result of a defending team's infraction.
- **24.2 Procedure.** Referee announces the player; puck is placed on the **center face-off spot**; on the Referee's whistle the shooter plays the puck from there and attempts to score. **The puck must be kept in motion toward the opponent's goal line**; once shot, the play is complete. **No goal can be scored on a rebound** (exception: off the post or crossbar then off the goalkeeper and directly in). Anytime the puck crosses the goal line or comes to a complete stop, the shot is complete. **The lacrosse-like move** whereby the puck is picked up on the blade of the stick (like in lacrosse) is permitted as long as the puck is shot toward the goal and no actions are dishonorable; cradling above shoulder height is not permitted. Only a designated goalkeeper or alternate goalkeeper may defend a penalty shot. The goalkeeper **must remain in his crease** until the shooter has touched the puck. If the goalkeeper was removed when the shot was awarded, he is permitted to return before the shot. The penalized team may replace its goalie to defend, but the substitute must remain in the game until the next stoppage. Other players from both sides withdraw to the sides of the rink in front of their own benches.
- **24.3 Designated Player.** When a penalty shot is awarded to a specifically fouled player, that player takes the shot. Otherwise the Captain of the non-offending team selects from players on the ice when the infraction was committed (cannot be changed). If injury prevents the designated shooter, the Captain selects from those on the ice when the infraction occurred. If the designated shooter himself commits an infraction during/around the same play, he takes the shot first, then serves the penalty — unless it is a game misconduct or match (then someone else shoots).
- **24.4 Violations During the Shot.** Goalkeeper attempting a save may not throw his stick or any object, or deliberately dislodge the goal (= awarded goal). If the goal becomes dislodged accidentally while attempting a save: (i) award a goal if the puck would have entered the area normally occupied by the net; (ii) allow re-take if it cannot be determined; (iii) if dislodged after the puck crossed the goal line, the shot is complete. If a player/coach/non-playing personnel of the opposing team interferes with/distracts the shooter and the shot fails, a second attempt is permitted; offending team gets a bench minor (player on bench responsible = misconduct on the player; coach or non-playing personnel = automatic suspension from the game, report to Commissioner). If a player on the team taking the shot interferes/distracts the goalkeeper and the shot is successful, **no goal**; bench minor to the offending team. Spectator interference: re-take. If a stick is ruled illegal and the second attempt's stick is also illegal, the shot is disallowed; the player is assessed a minor for the first illegal stick.
- **24.5 Face-Off Location.** Goal: face-off at center ice. No goal: face-off at one of the end face-off spots in the zone in which the penalty shot was attempted, except where another rule dictates an alternate (e.g., point men in too far, attacking team penalized on same play — Rule 76.2).
- **24.6 Results.** A goal on a penalty shot does not eliminate associated majors/matches/misconducts (those are still served). If the underlying offense was a minor, **no further minor is served** regardless of outcome. If a double-minor was the underlying offense, only the second minor is assessed and served (the first restored the lost opportunity). **No penalty expires on a goal scored on a penalty shot.** Two penalty shots awarded to the same team at the same stoppage: only one goal can be scored at the stoppage; if the first shot scores, the second is not taken but the penalty for the second infraction is served.
- **24.7 Timing.** If the infraction occurred during actual playing time, the shot is awarded and taken immediately even if regulation time has expired. The time required for the shot is not included in the regular playing time or overtime.
- **24.8 Infractions.** See Table 11. **Four conditions** for a penalty shot for being fouled (Rule 57.3):
  - (i) Infraction must have taken place in the neutral or attacking zone (over the puck carrier's own blue line);
  - (ii) Infraction must have been committed **from behind**;
  - (iii) Player in possession and control (or who clearly would have obtained it) must have been denied a **reasonable chance to score** (the fact a shot was got off does not eliminate consideration);
  - (iv) Player must have had **no opposing player between himself and the goalkeeper**.

**In practice:** Penalty shots are rare; most "breakaway fouls" are simply minor penalties because one of the four conditions fails.

### Rule 25 — Awarded Goals

*When the cheating is so blatant during a 6-on-5 push that the lost goal is granted automatically.*

- **25.1 Awarded Goal.** A goal is awarded to the attacking team when the opposing team has taken its goalkeeper off the ice and an attacking player has possession and control (or would have gained it) in the neutral or attacking zone, **without a defending player between him and the goal**, and is prevented from scoring by a defending team's infraction (see 25.3).
- **25.2 Infractions — When Goalkeeper is On the Ice.** A goal is awarded when an attacking player with an imminent scoring opportunity is prevented from scoring as a result of a defending player or goalkeeper **displacing the goal post** (deliberately or accidentally).
- **25.3 Infractions — When Goalkeeper is Off the Ice.** See Table 12 (Delay of game, Handling puck, Illegal substitution, Interference, Leaving the bench, Throwing stick, Tripping/fouling from behind).
- **25.4 Infractions — During the Course of a Penalty Shot.** A goal is awarded when a goalkeeper attempts to stop a penalty shot by throwing his stick/any object at the shooter, or by dislodging the goal (deliberately or accidentally) (see Rule 63.7).

**In practice:** "Awarded goal" — almost always paired with the empty-net scenario, where a defender desperate to break up an open-net chance commits a foul that would otherwise be a penalty.

### Rule 26 — Delayed Penalties

*When a team already has 3 in the box, the next penalty's clock waits until one expires.*

- **26.1 Delayed Penalties.** When a third player on the same team is sent to the penalty box, his time does not start until one of the others' time has elapsed; meanwhile a substitute for the third offender is on the ice (so the team plays only two skaters short at most).
- **26.2 Penalty Expiration.** When a team has three players serving simultaneously (and a substitute for the third is on the ice), none of the three may return until play has stopped, **at which point the player whose penalty has fully expired may return**. During play the Penalty Timekeeper releases players in the order of expiration **only when the penalized team is entitled to have more than four players on the ice**. Otherwise they wait until the first stoppage after their penalties expire. When two penalties of the same team expire simultaneously, the Captain designates which player returns first.
- **26.3 Major and Minor Penalty.** When a major and a minor are imposed at the same time on different players of the same team, the Penalty Timekeeper records the **minor as the first penalty**.

**In practice:** No team ever plays 3-on-5 or worse in real time; the rule guarantees a minimum of three skaters.

### Rule 27 — Goalkeeper's Penalties

*Goalkeepers don't sit; a teammate serves the time on their behalf.*

- **27.1 Minor Penalty to Goalkeeper.** Never serves the time; another teammate on the ice at the stoppage (designated by Coach through Captain) serves it. **A penalized player may not serve a goalkeeper's penalty.** If the goalkeeper is involved in coincidental penalties causing extra short-handed time, the Coach designates any player to serve the extra time.
- **27.2 Major Penalty to Goalkeeper.** Same principle — a teammate serves; **three majors in one game** = ruled off (the goalie's place is taken by an available replacement with full equipment, i.e., major + game misconduct).
- **27.3 Misconduct Penalty to Goalkeeper.** Served by another teammate on the ice when play was stopped.
- **27.4 Game Misconduct Penalty to Goalkeeper.** Replacement (member of the Club or substitute goalkeeper) takes his place with full equipment.
- **27.5 Match Penalty to Goalkeeper.** Replacement takes his place with full equipment. Time served by a designated teammate on the ice at the time. If coincidental with a match/major to opposing team, no player needs to proceed to the penalty bench to serve the goalie's match.
- **27.6 Leaving Goal Crease.** A goalkeeper who leaves the **immediate vicinity** of his crease during an altercation = minor + reported to the Commissioner. (If the altercation is in/near the crease, the Referee directs the goalie to a neutral location and no penalty.) No penalty if he is legitimately outside the crease to head to the bench for an extra attacker and becomes involved in an altercation.
- **27.7 Participating in the Play Over the Center Red Line.** A goalkeeper participating in the play over the center red line = minor for delay of game. During stoppages he may not proceed to his bench for a stick/equipment/instructions without permission from the Referee; otherwise replaced immediately or a bench minor for delay of game.
- **27.8 Restricted Area.** A goalkeeper shall not play the puck **outside the designated trapezoid area** behind the net (6' from each post extending diagonally to points 28' apart at the end boards). Minor for delay of game; determining factor is the position of the puck. **No penalty** when the goalkeeper plays the puck while maintaining skate contact with his crease.
- **27.9 Infractions — Unique to Goalkeepers.** See Table 13.

**In practice:** The trapezoid was added in 2005 to curb puck-handling goalies who could effectively act as a third defenseman behind the net.

### Rule 28 — Supplementary Discipline

*The Commissioner can investigate any incident and add fines/suspensions on top of game penalties.*

- **28.1 Supplementary Discipline.** Beyond automatic fines/suspensions, the Commissioner may at his discretion investigate any incident in any pre-season, exhibition, league or playoff game and assess additional fines/suspensions for offenses by any player, goalkeeper, Trainer, Manager, Coach or non-playing Club personnel/executive — **whether or not the offense was penalized by the Referee**. Investigation requested by a Club or initiated by the League must begin within **24 hours** of the game's completion.
- **28.2 Pre-Season and Exhibition Games.** Suspensions imposed for pre-season/exhibition infractions are scheduled at the Commissioner's discretion so no team is short more players in a regular-league game than it would have been if the infraction occurred during the regular season.

**In practice:** This is the source of the Department of Player Safety's authority to add suspensions/fines for hits and incidents missed (or only penalized partially) on the ice.

## Section 5 — Officials (Rules 30–40)

### Rule 30 — Appointment of Officials

*The Commissioner appoints all referees, linespersons, the Video Goal Judge and all off-ice officials.*

- **30.1 Appointment of Officials by Commissioner.** The Commissioner appoints all on-ice and off-ice officials for each game. The Commissioner forwards a list of officials to all Clubs, all of whom must be treated with proper respect at all times by players and Club officials.

**In practice:** NHL teams cannot select or veto officials; assignments come from the league.

### Rule 31 — Referees

*Two referees per game. They have general supervision, call penalties, rule on goals, and may consult the Linespersons.*

- **31.1 Attire and Equipment.** Black pants, NHL officials' sweaters/jerseys, League-approved black helmet with visor, approved whistles.
- **31.2 Disputes.** The Referees have **general supervision** of the game and **full control** of all game officials and players. Their decisions on disputes are final. As there is a human factor in blowing the whistle, the Referee may deem play to be stopped slightly prior to the whistle being blown — the fact that the puck may come loose or cross the goal line just before the audible whistle has no bearing if the Referee has ruled play stopped. Disputes about time/penalty expiration are referred to the Referee for adjustment (final); he may use the Video Goal Judge/Situation Room to assist (Rule 37.6).
- **31.3 Face-offs.** A Referee faces off the puck to start each period. **Linespersons handle all other face-offs**, including the start of regular-season overtime.
- **31.4 General Duties.** Referees impose penalties prescribed by the rules and give the final on-ice decision on disputed goals (may consult Linespersons first). Referees **do not call off-sides (Rule 83) or icings (Rule 81)** — those are Linespersons' duties — unless a Linesperson is prevented from doing so by some unforeseen issue.
- **31.5 Goals.** Announced over the PA; reason for any disallowed goal announced at the first stoppage of play (regardless of any standard signal already given). Referee reports the goal scorer's name/number to the In-Arena Scorer but does **not** give any information on assists (which the In-Arena Scorer awards via video replay).
- **31.6 Off-Ice Officials.** Before starting the game, ensure off-ice officials including the Game Timekeeper are in place and the timing/signaling equipment is in order.
- **31.7 Penalties.** Each penalty is announced correctly over the PA. **Visiting player's penalty announced first** when both teams are penalized on the same play. Mandatory/automatic fines: only the time portion is reported and announced; fines collected through the League office.
- **31.8 Players' Uniforms.** Referees ensure compliance.
- **31.9 Reports.** Promptly and in detail to the Commissioner the circumstances surrounding: (i) game misconducts; (ii) match penalties; (iii) the assessment of an instigator penalty; (iv) any time a goalkeeper leaves his crease during an altercation; (v) any time a stick/object is thrown outside the playing area; (vi) any time a player/goalkeeper/non-playing personnel is involved in an altercation with a spectator; (vii) any unusual on/off ice occurrence.
- **31.10 Start and End of Game and Periods.** Order teams onto the ice at the appointed time. If more than 15 minutes delay or undue intermission delay, state in the report the cause and the Club(s) at fault. Remain on the ice at the end of each period until all players have proceeded to their dressing rooms. Check rosters and uniformed players before signing the Official Report of Match.
- **31.11 Unable to Continue.** Should a Referee leave the ice or be incapacitated during play, **the game is automatically stopped**. The game continues using one Referee + two Linespersons. If through illness/accident one Referee is unable to officiate, the remaining Referee assumes the ill Referee's duties; League Hockey Operations/Officiating personnel in attendance may substitute the spare official. If all on-ice officials cannot appear and replacements cannot be found, the Managers/Coaches agree on Referees and Linespersons; if unable to agree, each side appoints one player (home Club's = Referee, visiting Club's = Linesperson). When the regular officials arrive, they at once replace the temporary officials.

**In practice:** The "two referee + two linesperson" system has been standard since 2000. A Referee's call on goalie interference is final unless overturned via a Coach's Challenge.

### Rule 32 — Linespersons

*Two linespersons per game. They handle off-sides, icing, face-offs, and stop play for many specific situations.*

- **32.1 Attire and Equipment.** Black pants, NHL officials' sweaters/jerseys, League-approved black helmet with visor, approved whistles.
- **32.2 Face-offs.** The Linesperson conducts face-offs at all times except to start each period (Referee).
- **32.3 General Duties.** Generally responsible for **off-sides (Rule 83) and icing (Rule 81)** calls. May stop play for many other situations (32.4–32.5).
- **32.4 Reporting to Referee.** The Linesperson **may** stop play and report what he witnessed:
  - (i) Too many men (Rule 74);
  - (ii) Articles thrown on the ice from the players' or penalty bench (Rule 75);
  - (iii) Team personnel interfering with a game official (Rule 39);
  - (iv) A player who lost/broke his stick and receives one illegally (Rule 10);
  - (v) Identifiable player on the bench whose stick/body interferes with the puck or an opponent (Rule 56).
  - Must report after play: (vi) Major; (vii) Match; (viii) Misconduct; (ix) Game Misconduct; (x) Abuse of Officials (Rule 39); (xi) Physical Abuse of Officials (Rule 40); (xii) Unsportsmanlike Conduct (Rule 75).
  - If a Linesperson witnesses such an infraction by an attacking player (undetected by Referees) prior to an attacking goal, he reports it to the Referees, the goal is disallowed and the penalty assessed.
- **32.5 Stopping Play.** The Linesperson **must** stop play immediately:
  - (i) Premature substitution (Rule 71);
  - (ii) Serious injury undetected by Referees (Rule 8);
  - (iii) Encroachment into the face-off area (Rule 76);
  - (iv) Puck directed with a hand to a teammate in any zone other than the defending zone, undetected by Referees (Rule 79);
  - (v) Puck batted with the hand by either center in an attempt to win the face-off (Rule 76);
  - (vi) Puck struck above shoulder height with a stick undetected by Referees (Rule 80);
  - (vii) Icing (Rule 81);
  - (viii) Spectator interference (Rule 24);
  - (ix) Off-side at the blue line (Rule 83);
  - (x) Puck out of bounds or unplayable (Rule 85);
  - (xi) Goal not observed by the Referees (Rule 78);
  - (xii) Puck interfered with by an ineligible player/person (Rules 5, 74, 78, 84);
  - (xiii) The calling of a penalty shot (Rule 53).
- **32.6 Unable to Continue.** If a Linesperson can't act and no replacement is available, the two Referees assist while still able to assess penalties.

**In practice:** Linesmen are the de facto stewards of the procedural rules; refs handle penalties.

### Rule 33 — In-Arena Scorer

*Off-ice official who lists eligible players, awards goals and assists, and keeps the score sheet.*

- **33.1 General Duties.** Lists all eligible players and the starting line-up; indicates "C" or "A" opposite Captains' and Alternates' names; keeps the record of goals scored, the scorer, and the player(s) entitled to an assist (max 2).
- **33.2 Goals and Assists.** Awards goals/assists using the Video Replay system; awards announced twice over the PA; all changes also announced twice. At the conclusion of the game, completes and signs the Score Sheet and forwards it (with the Official Report of Match and Penalty Record) to the League office. Scoring change requests must come from a team representative within **24 hours**. **A goal is awarded to the last player on the scoring team to touch the puck prior to the puck entering the net.** A **maximum of two assists** are awarded; an assisting player must have touched the puck prior to the scorer with no defender having played or possessed the puck in between. Final-minute goals (clock showing tenths) are rounded up to the nearest second for the records.
- **33.3 Line-ups.** Files a report to the Commissioner (Toronto office) if either Coach fails to comply with line-up submission rules (Rule 5.1).
- **33.4 Location.** Elevated position, away from benches, with PA telephone, TV monitor + recording, and access to the Video Goal Judge.
- **33.5 Penalties.** Assists the Penalty Timekeeper. May stop play for ineligible-player situations.

**In practice:** This is why goals/assists "officially" appear on the broadcast a few seconds after the play; the In-Arena Scorer is reviewing replay.

### Rule 34 — Game Timekeeper

*Runs the game clock and intermission clock; synchronizes with the TV producer.*

- **34.1 General Duties.** Starts the clock on the puck drop, stops on whistle or goal. Announces the one-minute warning at 19:00 of each period.
- **34.2 Intermissions.** Uses the electronic clock to display intermission time. Intermissions are **eighteen minutes (18:00)** unless otherwise notified. Clock starts once the last official exits the ice. Discretion if there are unusual delays (altercation, building/ice issues).
- **34.3 Overtime.** In regular-season OT, resets the clock to **5:00**; OT begins promptly after the entire ice surface is shoveled. In playoffs, each intermission is completed in a normal manner.
- **34.4 Signal Devices.** If no automatic device or it fails, signals end of each period by blowing a whistle.
- **34.5 Start of Periods.** Signals Referees and teams for start of game and each succeeding period; Referees start play promptly per Rule 77.
- **34.6 Television.** Synchronizes timing with the TV producer of the originating broadcast.
- **34.7 Verification of Time.** Any loss of time due to a false face-off must be replaced. May consult Video Goal Judge. Disputes referred to Referees (decision final); the Game Timekeeper assists with a stopwatch. If the clock fails when play resumes, on-ice officials may stop play unless there is imminent scoring or wait for the next stoppage. If video replay shows a goal scored before play was stopped, the Video Goal Judge informs the Game Timekeeper and In-Arena Scorer of the time of the goal and the amount of playing time to reset on the clock.

**In practice:** This is the off-ice official whose precise timing keeps the broadcast in sync with the game.

### Rule 35 — Penalty Timekeeper

*Tracks every penalty (player, type, duration, time imposed); operates the penalty clock; verifies expiration times.*

- **35.1 General Duties.** Maintains the Penalty Record. Informs penalized players and Penalty Box Attendants of correct expiration times. The penalty time clock is the **official timing device** for any penalty that requires playing with fewer than five skaters; if it fails, the game clock is used. Players are released only when indicated by the penalty time clock or as covered in the rules. Penalties are announced **twice** over the PA; visiting player's penalty announced first when both teams are penalized at the same time. For multiple game misconducts on the same player at the same stoppage, only one is announced. **Misconduct penalties and coincident major penalties are NOT recorded on the penalty time clock**; those players are alerted and released at the first stoppage after expiration. A misconduct combined with other penalties begins only after all other penalties have been served (or washed out by a goal).
- **35.2 Equipment.** Has an official stick-measuring gauge and tape measure available for the Referees.
- **35.3 Goalkeeper's Penalties.** Served by another teammate on the ice when play was stopped; the Penalty Timekeeper coordinates with In-Arena Scorer/Real Time Scorers to ensure only an eligible player serves the time.
- **35.4 Penalty Shot.** Records each penalty shot awarded (shooter, result).
- **35.5 Penalty Time Clock.** Responsible for correct posting; promptly calls discrepancies to the Referees. When two players from one team and one from the other are penalized simultaneously, requests through the Referee or offending Captain which penalty to display.
- **35.6 Reports.** Prior to each game, obtains the Instigator, Aggressor & Game Misconduct List from the League. After the game, completes and signs the Penalty Record and forwards it. Final-minute penalties (clock showing tenths) are rounded up to the nearest second. When a player is ejected, completes an "Off-Ice Officials Report of Game Misconduct/Match Penalties."
- **35.7 Stick Measurements.** Records the result of each measurement on the Penalty Record.

**In practice:** This is the off-ice official who runs the penalty box clock — the most-watched timer in hockey after the main clock.

### Rule 36 — Real Time Scorers

*A team of off-ice officials who record every official statistic electronically.*

- **36.1 General Duties.** Electronically record all official statistics per League instructions.
- **36.2 Real Time Scorers.** Each game has: (i) Stats Entry Scorer (3); (ii) Time on Ice Scorer; (iii) Event Analyst. League-assigned **Scoring System Manager (SSM)** oversees and works one of the five positions.
- **36.3 Reports.** Generated during intermissions and post-game; distributed by the SSM (or designate) to the home club's PR rep, who distributes to media and to each Club's Coaches.

**In practice:** This crew is responsible for every modern hockey stat: time on ice, shot location, hit logging, faceoff wins, etc.

### Rule 37 — Video Review

*The NHL Situation Room in Toronto reviews every goal, and certain other potential goals/non-goals. Its decision is final.*

- **37.1 NHL Situation Room/Video Goal Judge.** The NHL staffs a centralized Situation Room with access to all broadcast replays, with direct/immediate access to penalty-bench off-ice officials and on-ice officials.
- **37.2 Goal Review Procedures.** **Every goal is reviewed by the NHL Situation Room.** If there is a need to delay the center-ice face-off following an apparent goal, the off-ice official at ice level alerts a Referee to delay. Once confirmed, the official at ice level signals to resume play. If an expanded review is required, the Situation Room sounds the in-arena horn and the PA announces *"play is under review."* If the Situation Room observes an undetected potential goal: contacts the Referee at the next stoppage; PA announces play is under review; if a goal is awarded, the clock (including penalty clocks if applicable) is reset to the time of the goal. **Only one goal can be awarded at any stoppage of play.** Any potential goal requiring review must be reviewed prior to and/or during the next stoppage — **no goal may be awarded (or disallowed) once play has resumed**. The Situation Room's decision is final.
- **37.3 Goal Situations Subject to Video Review.** The following are subject to review by the NHL Situation Room:
  - (a) Puck crossing the goal line;
  - (b) Puck in the net prior to the goal frame being dislodged;
  - (c) Puck in the net prior to (or after) the expiration of time at the end of a period;
  - (d) With the use of a foot/skate, was a **"distinct kicking motion"** evident?;
  - (e) Puck deliberately directed, batted, or thrown into the net by an attacking player by any means (and any part of his body) other than with his stick;
  - (f) Puck deflected directly into the net off an On-Ice Official;
  - (g) Puck struck or deflected into the net with a high-stick **above the height of the crossbar**;
  - (h) Puck entering the net in a proper manner through the goal mouth (ensuring the puck did not enter improperly through net meshing or underneath the net frame);
  - (i) Puck entering the net as the culmination of a continuous play where the result was unaffected by any whistle blown by the Referee upon losing sight of the puck;
  - (j) The legitimacy of all potential goals on Penalty Shot or Shootout attempts.
- **37.4 Distinct Kicking Motion.** A puck entering the net as a direct result of a "distinct kicking motion" is **NO GOAL**. A distinct kicking motion is one where the video makes clear the attacking player **deliberately propelled the puck with a kick** and it subsequently entered the net (including via a subsequent deflection off another player). Kicking any equipment (stick, glove, helmet, etc.) at the puck — including kicking the blade of one's own stick — to cause the puck to cross the line: **NO GOAL**. A puck that deflects into the net off an attacking player's skate **without** a distinct kicking motion is a **GOAL**. A puck directed into the net by a skate is also a **GOAL** as long as no distinct kicking motion is evident (see Rule 49.2).
- **37.5 Puck Struck with a High Stick.** **Determining factor: where the puck makes contact with the stick relative to the crossbar.** If contact is at or below the level of the crossbar (despite some other portion of the stick being above), and it enters the goal, **goal is allowed**.
- **37.6 Video Review to Verify Time on Clock.** The Situation Room may use video review to establish the correct time on the clock. The "burn-in" of the game clock on the two overhead goal-cam feeds is mandatory, and made available on as many other feeds as possible. **Any loss of time on the game or penalty clocks due to a false face-off, face-off violation, or puck going out of play must be replaced.** The Situation Room may be consulted or may intervene directly. Disputes go to the Situation Room (final). Game Timekeeper assists with a stopwatch. If video review shows a goal was scored before play was stopped, the Situation Room informs the Game Timekeeper and In-Arena Scorer of the goal time and amount of playing time to reset on the game/penalty clocks. If the Situation Room can determine a goal via replay while play has continued, the horn is sounded to stop play and the goal awarded; clock is reset.

**In practice:** Every goal in the NHL is reviewed in Toronto in near-real time; close ones see the on-ice officials briefly pause the next face-off until the Situation Room confirms.

### Rule 38 — Coach's Challenge

*A coach can challenge four specific scenarios. Unsuccessful challenges cost a 2-minute (4-minute for repeat) delay-of-game penalty.*

- **38.1 General.** Coach's Challenge can only be utilized in **GOAL/NO GOAL** situations and **delay of game – puck over glass** situations. It is intended to be extremely narrow. The on-ice call is overturned **only if a conclusive and irrefutable determination** can be made on the basis of video evidence that the original call was clearly not correct. If not conclusive or any doubt remains, the original call is confirmed.
- **38.2 Situations Subject to Coach's Challenge.** A team may only request a Coach's Challenge to review:
  - **(a) "Off-Side" Play Leading to a Goal** — a play resulting in a GOAL where the defending team claims the play should have been stopped for off-side (Rule 83);
  - **(b) Missed Game Stoppage Event in the Offensive Zone Leading to a Goal** — a play resulting in a GOAL where the defending team claims play should have been stopped by reason of any play occurring in the offensive zone that should have resulted in a play stoppage caused by the attacking team but did not. (*Exception:* puck striking the spectator netting caused by either team and unnoticed by on-ice officials.) Potential stoppages include but are not limited to: Hand Pass (Rule 79); High-sticking the Puck (Rule 80); Puck Out of Bounds (Rule 85). Such infractions only overturn a GOAL if video review **conclusively** establishes a missed stoppage event. **Missed penalty calls subject to discretion/judgment cannot overturn a GOAL.**
  - **(c) Scoring Plays Involving Potential "Interference on the Goalkeeper"** — either:
    - (i) GOAL on ice where the defending team claims the goal should have been disallowed for goalie interference (Rules 69.1, 69.3, 69.4); or
    - (ii) NO GOAL on ice (despite the puck entering the net), where the attacking team claims:
      - (A) there was **no actual contact** initiated by an attacker with the goalkeeper; or
      - (B) the attacking player was **pushed, shoved or fouled** by a defending player causing him to contact the goalkeeper; or
      - (C) the attacking player's positioning within the goal crease **did not impair the goalkeeper's ability** to defend his goal and had **no discernable impact** on the play.
  - **(d) Penalty situations for "Delaying the game – puck over the glass"** — a minor for puck-over-glass call to a defending player, where the team claims the puck was deflected/rebounded off the boards, glass, goal net frame or otherwise.
- **38.3** (Threshold matter: Hockey Operations must first determine the puck legally entered the net before any "Off-Side," "Missed Game Stoppage" or "Interference on the Goalkeeper" Challenge proceeds. If Rule 37 review renders a Challenge unnecessary, the Challenge is deemed not to have been made.)
- **38.4 Process for Initiating a Coach's Challenge.** Verbally communicated to a Referee:
  - For a GOAL: **before the center-ice face-off**;
  - For NO GOAL on goalie interference, or for the puck-over-glass minor: **before the resumption of play**.
  - Teams may not unduly delay; delay tactics may result in denial of the right + (Referee's discretion) a minor for delaying the game (Rule 63). Coaches must provide, **with reasonable specificity**, both the actual infraction claimed and the approximate clock time when it occurred — failure may result in denial. **Only one Coach's Challenge per team per stoppage is permitted.**
- **38.5 Process for Reviewing a Coach's Challenge.** Each arena has technology (tablet/monitor) for on-ice officials to review replays alongside the Situation Room. To the extent practical, on-ice replays match Situation Room replays. The Situation Room contacts the Referee responsible for the call (or non-call) via headset and discusses (i) the Referee's final call on ice and (ii) what was observed before any video is shown. The Situation Room is staffed with at least one former Referee for all games to assist (regular season and playoffs). On-ice and Situation Room review simultaneously, then the Situation Room renders the final decision.
- **38.6 Right to Initiate a Coach's Challenge.** Teams may initiate during the game **except during the final minute of regulation** or **at any time during overtime (regular season and playoffs)**, at which point the exclusive right to initiate review shifts to the **NHL Situation Room**.
- **38.7 Results of a Successful Coach's Challenge.** **Off-Side / Missed Game Stoppage Event:** goal disallowed; clock reset to the time at which play should have been stopped; face-off occurs where it would have if the call had been made properly. **Penalties assessed between the missed infraction and the disallowance still stand**, with their times recorded as when play should have been stopped. **Interference on the Goalkeeper:** goal disallowed; face-off at the nearest neutral zone face-off spot **outside the attacking zone**. The review is used **exclusively to overturn the GOAL call** — not for assessing minor or major penalties for goalkeeper interference.
- **38.8 Results of an Unsuccessful Coach's Challenge.** **First unsuccessful Challenge: minor (2:00) for delaying the game.** For a team that has already had one or more unsuccessful Challenges in the game, an additional unsuccessful Challenge = **double-minor (4:00) for delaying the game**.
- **38.9 Applicable Standards for "Off-Side" Challenge.** The standard for overturning a GOAL call: the Situation Room determines, after reviewing all available replays and consulting on-ice officials, that **one or more attacking players preceded the puck into the attacking zone prior to the goal and the play should have been stopped**. Goals are only subject to review for potential off-side **if the puck did not come out of the attacking zone again between the time of the off-side and the time the goal was scored**.
- **38.10 Applicable Standards for "Missed Game Stoppage Event in the Offensive Zone" Challenge.** Standard for overturning: the Situation Room determines the play should have been stopped (but was not) at some point after the puck entered the attacking zone but prior to the goal.
- **38.11 Applicable Standards for Potential "Interference on the Goalkeeper" Challenge.** To overturn a GOAL: Situation Room determines the goal should have been disallowed per Rules 69.1, 69.3, 69.4. To overturn a NO GOAL: Situation Room determines goal should have been allowed because either (i) no contact initiated by attacker, (ii) attacker was pushed/shoved/fouled into the goalie, or (iii) attacker's positioning did not impair the goalie's ability and had no impact.
- **38.12 Applicable Standards for "Delaying the Game – Puck Over Glass" Challenge.** Standard: Situation Room determines the puck was deflected/rebounded off the boards, glass, goal net frame or otherwise. If so, the minor is rescinded and the face-off conducted in the defending zone.

**In practice:** The puck-over-glass challenge was added because the bench-side angle is often deceiving — the puck can hit the glass first and deflect over, which is *not* a penalty.

### Rule 39 — Abuse of Officials

*Yelling, gesturing, banging boards, or persistent disputing escalates: minor → misconduct → game misconduct.*

- **39.1 General Description.** No player, goalkeeper, Coach or non-playing person shall challenge or dispute an official's ruling before, during or after a game, or display unsportsmanlike conduct including obscene, profane, or abusive language or gestures, comments of a personal nature intended to degrade an official, or persist in disputing a ruling after being told to stop or after being penalized. **When such conduct is directed at anyone other than an official, Rule 75 applies.**
- **39.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for unsportsmanlike conduct:
  - (i) Any player who challenges or disputes the ruling of an official.
  - (ii) Any identifiable player who uses obscene/profane/abusive language or gestures directed at any on/off-ice official.
  - (iii) Any player(s) who bang the boards with sticks or other objects, or who otherwise show disrespect for an official's decision (Referees use discretion if it is to get attention for a legitimate reason like serious injury).
  - (iv) When a Captain, Alternate Captain or any other player comes off the bench to question or protest a ruling.
  - (v) If a player bangs the glass in protest of an Off-Ice Official's ruling (persistence = misconduct).
  - (vi) An additional unsportsmanlike penalty to a penalized player is **added to his unexpired time and served consecutively**.
- **39.3 Bench Minor Penalty.** Bench minor for unsportsmanlike conduct:
  - (i) Coach/non-playing person banging boards with stick or other object showing disrespect (Referee discretion for legitimate reasons).
  - (ii) Unidentifiable player or any Coach/non-playing person who uses obscene/profane/abusive language or gesture directed at an on/off-ice official, or uses an official's name coupled with vociferous remarks.
  - (iii) Any player/Coach/non-playing person interfering with any game official in performance of duties.
- **39.4 Misconduct Penalty.** For:
  - (i) Persisting in obscene/profane/abusive language for which already assessed a minor/bench minor.
  - (ii) Intentionally knocking/shooting the puck out of the reach of an official retrieving it.
  - (iii) Persists in challenging/disputing rulings after an unsportsmanlike minor.
  - (iv) Player(s) banging boards after already assessed a minor or bench minor.
  - (v) Except for taking their positions, players leaving the bench to dispute an official's ruling.
  - (vi) Any penalized player who does not proceed directly to the penalty bench when instructed.
  - (vii) A misconduct (or game misconduct at Referee discretion) on situational behaviour.
  - (viii) Any player who, after previously assessed a minor for banging the glass, persists.
- **39.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** For:
  - (i) Persisting in challenging/disputing rulings after a misconduct.
  - (ii) Coach/non-playing person using obscene/profane/abusive language/gesture or naming an official with vociferous remarks after already being assessed a bench minor (39.3(ii)). For post-game conduct, game misconduct applies without prior bench minor.
  - (iii) Deliberately applying physical force to an official, attempting to injure an official, physically demeaning, or applying force to get free of an official during/after an altercation (apply Rule 40).
  - (iv) Entering the penalty bench and leaving prior to expiration to challenge a ruling = game misconduct + automatic **three-game** suspension (regular and/or playoff games). Does not replace any more severe penalty (Rule 70).
  - (v) Minor for unsportsmanlike conduct + game misconduct on a player who throws his stick (or any part/equipment/object) outside the playing area in protest of an official's decision.
  - (vi) Throwing/shooting any equipment in the general direction of an official without contact.
  - (vii) General escalation rule: minor → misconduct → game misconduct if behaviour persists.
- **39.6 Reports.** All game officials and Club officials file confidential reports on obscene gestures/language to the Commissioner for further discipline.

**In practice:** This is why visible swearing at refs results in escalating penalties — the rule scales by repetition.

### Rule 40 — Physical Abuse of Officials

*Three categories of physical contact with officials, with mandatory minimum suspensions of 20 / 10 / 3 games.*

- **40.1 Game Misconduct.** Any player who deliberately applies physical force to an official, attempts to injure an official, physically demeans an official, or deliberately applies physical force to an official solely to get free during/after an altercation = **game misconduct** + categories below.
- **40.2 Automatic Suspension — Category I.** Deliberately strikes an official and causes injury, or applies physical force with intent to injure, or attempts to injure an official: **not less than 20 games**. "Intent to injure" = any physical force which a player knew or should have known could reasonably be expected to cause injury.
- **40.3 Automatic Suspension — Category II.** Deliberately applies physical force without intent to injure (or **spits on** an official): **not less than 10 games**.
- **40.4 Automatic Suspension — Category III.** Physically demeans or threatens an official by throwing a stick/equipment/object at or in the general direction of an official, shooting the puck at or near an official, spitting at or near an official, or applying physical force solely to get free during/after an altercation: **not less than 3 games**.
- **40.5 Automatic Suspension — Process.** Immediately after the game, the Referees (consulting with Linespersons) decide the category, make verbal and written reports to Hockey Operations, and may request a review of suspension length. NHLPA, player and Club are notified the morning after. League holds a conference call with the NHLPA to review the application; no public comment until that call. Player or officials may request the Commissioner review; hearing is expedited (best efforts to hear before the player's second missed game). The suspension continues pending the outcome. **Category III may be conducted by telephone**; Category I and II hearings in person. The Commissioner may: sustain the minimum, increase within the category, change to a lower category (with or without an increase in games), or — Category III only — reduce the number of games.
- **40.6 Supplementary Discipline.** Multiple offenses under this rule may be referred to the Commissioner for additional discipline.
- **40.7 Coach or Non-Playing Club Personnel.** Any Manager/Coach/non-playing personnel who holds or strikes an official is automatically suspended from the game, ordered to the dressing room, and reported to the Commissioner for further discipline.
- **40.8 Police Protection and Security.** Clubs must provide adequate police or other protection for all players, goalkeepers and officials. Failures reported to the Commissioner.

**In practice:** These suspensions are among the longest automatic discipline in the game — physical abuse of officials sits in a special category of misconduct.

## Section 6 — Physical Infractions (Rules 41–53)

### Rule 41 — Boarding

*Checking a defenseless opponent so he is propelled violently into the boards.*

- **41.1 Boarding.** A boarding penalty is imposed on any player who **checks or pushes a defenseless opponent in such a manner that causes the opponent to hit or impact the boards violently or dangerously**. Severity based on impact is at the Referee's discretion. The **onus is on the player applying the check** to ensure his opponent is not in a defenseless position; if so, he must avoid or minimize contact. Considerations include whether the opponent put himself in a vulnerable position immediately prior to or simultaneously with the check or whether the check was unavoidable. **Unnecessary contact with a player playing the puck on an obvious icing or off-side play that results in board impact is boarding.** Where there is no board contact, the infraction is treated as charging.
- **41.2 Minor Penalty.** At Referee's discretion, based on impact severity.
- **41.3 Major Penalty.** At Referee's discretion, based on impact severity (see 41.5).
- **41.4 Match Penalty.** At Referee's discretion if the player attempted to or deliberately injured by boarding.
- **41.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** When a major is imposed under this rule for an infraction resulting in injury to the **face or head**, a game misconduct is automatic.
- **41.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6 — Physical Infractions Category. Supplementary discipline at the Commissioner's discretion (Rule 28).

**In practice:** "Was he in a defenseless position?" is the question that distinguishes a hard-but-legal hit from a boarding minor or major.

### Rule 42 — Charging

*Skating distance or jumping to deliver a violent check, including against a goalkeeper in the crease.*

- **42.1 Charging.** A minor or major shall be imposed on a player who skates, jumps into or charges an opponent **in any manner**. Charging = the actions of a player who, as a result of **distance traveled**, violently checks an opponent. A "charge" may be the result of a check into the boards, into the goal frame or in open ice. A minor, major or major + game misconduct shall be imposed on a player who **charges a goalkeeper while the goalkeeper is within his goal crease**. A goalkeeper is **not "fair game"** just because he is outside the goal crease — appropriate penalty whenever an opposing player makes unnecessary contact. Incidental contact permitted when the goalie is playing the puck outside the crease, provided the attacking player has made a reasonable effort to avoid contact.
- **42.2 Minor Penalty.** At Referee's discretion, based on degree of violence of the check.
- **42.3 Major Penalty.** At Referee's discretion, based on degree of violence (see 42.5).
- **42.4 Match Penalty.** At Referee's discretion if deliberate attempt to injure.
- **42.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Automatic when a major is imposed and there is an injury to the **face or head**.
- **42.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6. Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Charging differs from a clean body check in that the hit is delivered after a long, forceful runway.

### Rule 43 — Checking from Behind

*Hitting a defenseless player from behind. No minor exists — straight to major.*

- **43.1 Checking from Behind.** A check delivered on a player who is **not aware of the impending hit**, therefore unable to protect or defend himself, with contact made on the back part of the body. **When a player intentionally turns his body to create contact with his back, no penalty.**
- **43.2 Minor Penalty.** **No provision for a minor penalty for checking from behind.**
- **43.3 Major Penalty.** Any player who cross-checks, pushes or charges an opponent (causing them to hit something dangerously) from behind = major + game misconduct.
- **43.4 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury.
- **43.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Automatic with a major under this rule.
- **43.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6.

**In practice:** Because of the catastrophic neck-injury risk, the league removed the "minor" option entirely — every confirmed check-from-behind is at least a 5-minute major and an ejection.

### Rule 44 — Clipping

*A "knees" hit — throwing the body across or below an opponent's knees.*

- **44.1 Clipping.** The act of throwing the body **across or below the knees** of an opponent from any direction. A player may not deliver a check in a "clipping" manner, nor lower his body position to deliver a check on or below an opponent's knees. An illegal "low hit" is a check delivered by a player (with or without both skates on the ice) whose sole intent is to check the opponent in the area of his knees.
- **44.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for "clipping."
- **44.3 Major Penalty.** If injury results, a major must be assessed (see 44.5).
- **44.4 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury.
- **44.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Must be assessed any time a major is applied for injuring by clipping.
- **44.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6. Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Clipping is the formal name for "going low" — the league treats it as analogous to checking from behind in terms of injury risk.

### Rule 45 — Elbowing

*Using the elbow to illegally check an opponent.*

- **45.1 Elbowing.** Using the elbow in any way to foul an opponent.
- **45.2 Minor Penalty.** Referee's discretion, based on degree of violence.
- **45.3 Major Penalty.** Referee's discretion. **A major must be imposed for an infraction resulting in injury to the face or head** (see 45.5).
- **45.4 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury.
- **45.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Automatic with a major under this rule when injury to face/head.
- **45.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6.

**In practice:** Elbowing usually presents as a "flying elbow" extended into an opponent's head as he approaches a check.

### Rule 46 — Fighting

*Comprehensive set of rules covering aggressor, instigator, third-man-in, jersey tie-downs, fights off the playing surface, and fights other than during periods.*

- **46.1 Fighting.** A fight occurs when **at least one player punches or attempts to punch an opponent repeatedly, or two players wrestle in such a manner as to make it difficult for the Linespersons to intervene and separate the combatants**. Referees have very wide latitude in penalties so they can differentiate degrees of responsibility.
- **46.2 Aggressor.** The aggressor is the player who **continues to throw punches in an attempt to inflict punishment on his opponent who is in a defenseless position or who is an unwilling combatant**. Must be deemed aggressor when he has clearly won the fight but continues throwing and landing punches in a further attempt to inflict punishment/injury on an opponent no longer able to defend himself. Aggressor: **major for fighting + game misconduct**. Recorded for statistical and suspension purposes. A player deemed **both** instigator and aggressor = instigating minor + major for fighting + 10-minute misconduct (instigator) + game misconduct (aggressor).
- **46.3 Altercation.** A situation involving two players with at least one to be penalized.
- **46.4 Clearing the Area of a Fight.** All players not engaged go immediately to the area of their players' bench. If the altercation is at a players' bench, the players on the ice from that team go to their defending zone. Goalkeepers remain in their crease (unless the altercation is near it, then follow Referee directions). Non-compliance may result in penalties + fines (46.18).
- **46.5 Continuing or Attempting to Continue a Fight.** Game misconduct for any player who attempts to or persists in continuing an altercation.
- **46.6 Helmets.** **No player may remove his helmet prior to engaging in a fight.** If he does = minor (2:00) for unsportsmanlike conduct. Helmets that come off in the course of and resulting from the altercation do not result in a penalty.
- **46.7 Fighting After the Original Altercation.** Game misconduct for any player assessed a major for fighting after the original altercation has started. At Referee's discretion, the automatic game misconduct may be waived if the opposing player was clearly the instigator.
- **46.8 Fighting Off the Playing Surface.** Misconduct or game misconduct for any player involved in fighting off the playing surface or with another player who is off the playing surface (in addition to other time penalties). Coach or non-playing personnel involved with an opposing player/Coach on or off ice: automatic suspension from the game, ordered to the dressing room, reported to the Commissioner.
- **46.9 Fighting Other Than During the Periods of the Game.** Players involved in fighting other than during the periods are assessed a major + game misconduct. If one player is the instigator, the opponent does not get the game misconduct.
- **46.10 Fighting Prior to the Drop of the Puck.** Unless prior to the start of game or any period (46.9), a fight prior to a face-off is penalized as if it occurred during regular play.
- **46.11 Instigator.** A player who **by his actions or demeanor demonstrates any/some of the following**: distance traveled; gloves off first; first punch thrown; menacing attitude or posture; verbal instigation or threats; conduct in retaliation to a prior game (or season) incident; obvious retribution. Instigator: **instigating minor + major for fighting + 10-minute misconduct**. Same player a second time in the same game: instigating minor + major + **game misconduct**. **Third instigator penalty in one Regular season** = automatic game misconduct.
- **46.12 Instigator in Final Five Minutes of Regulation (or Anytime in Overtime).** Instigating minor + major for fighting + game misconduct (subject to 46.21).
- **46.13 Jerseys.** A player who deliberately removes his jersey prior to participating in an altercation = minor for unsportsmanlike + game misconduct. A player who engages in a fight and whose jersey is not properly **tied down** and loses his jersey (completely off his torso) = game misconduct. If the player loses the jersey despite the tie down remaining intact, the game misconduct is not applicable but the situation is reported to the League. No game misconduct for jersey removal when the opponent is identified as instigator and the player's jersey is removed by the opponent or an official.
- **46.14 Major Penalty.** Major for any player who fights.
- **46.15 Match Penalty.** Tape or other material on the hands (below the wrist) that cuts/injures an opponent during an altercation: match penalty in addition to fighting major. **"Sucker punch"** of an unsuspecting/unwilling opponent causing injury: match penalty.
- **46.16 Third Man In.** Game misconduct (at Referee's discretion) on **any player who is the first to intervene in an altercation already in progress** — except when a match is being imposed in the original altercation. This penalty is in addition to other penalties incurred. Applies to subsequent players who elect to intervene in the same or other altercations during the same stoppage.
- **46.17 Fines and Suspensions — Aggressor.** Third aggressor penalty in one Regular season = 2-game suspension. Fourth = 4 games. Fifth = 6 games. Playoffs: second aggressor = automatic 1 playoff game; each subsequent +1 game. Aggressor records reset before each Stanley Cup Final.
- **46.18** Fines for goalkeepers leaving the immediate vicinity of their crease during altercations: per Rule 27.6.
- **46.19 Fines and Suspensions — Fighting Other Than During the Periods of the Game.** Teams involved: $25,000 fine. Player deemed instigator at a time other than during the periods of the game: **automatic 10-game suspension**.
- **46.20 Fines and Suspensions — Instigator.** Third instigator penalty in one Regular season = 2-game suspension. Fourth = 4 games. Fifth = 6 games. Playoffs: second = automatic 1 playoff game; each subsequent +1 game. Reset before each Stanley Cup Final.
- **46.21 Fines and Suspensions — Instigator in Final Five Minutes of Regulation Time (or Anytime in Overtime).** **One-game suspension** pending review, **Coach fined $10,000** (doubles for each subsequent incident). Served unless Player Safety deems it is **not** related to the score, prior incidents, retaliation, "message sending," etc. Length doubles for each subsequent offense. In addition to any automatic suspensions for accumulated instigator penalties.

**In practice:** The instigator rule is what prevents "goons" from picking on top scorers in a one-sided fight; the third-man-in rule prevents brawls.

### Rule 47 — Head-butting

*Using the head as a weapon. Always at least a double-minor.*

- **47.1 Head-butting.** Using the head to strike an opponent.
- **47.2 Double-minor Penalty.** Imposed on a player who **attempts to head-butt** an opponent.
- **47.3 Major Penalty.** Imposed on a player who head-butts an opponent (see 47.5).
- **47.4 Match Penalty.** Imposed on a player who **injures** an opponent as a result of a head-butt.
- **47.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Mandatory when a major is assessed under this rule.
- **47.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6. Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Head-butting calls are rare in modern hockey, almost always seen in post-whistle scrums.

### Rule 48 — Illegal Check to the Head

*A check where, judged by three specific avoidability factors, contact with an opponent's head was avoidable. Minor only on the ice; supplementary discipline does the heavy lifting.*

- **48.1 Illegal Check to the Head.** In determining whether contact with an opponent's head was avoidable, the circumstances of the hit including the following shall be considered:
  - (i) Whether the player **attempted to hit squarely through the opponent's body** and the head was not "picked" as a result of poor timing, poor angle of approach, or unnecessary extension of the body upward or outward.
  - (ii) Whether the opponent put himself in a **vulnerable position** by assuming a posture that made head contact on an otherwise full body check unavoidable.
  - (iii) Whether the opponent materially **changed the position of his body or head** immediately prior to or simultaneously with the hit in a way that significantly contributed to the head contact.
- **48.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for violation.
- **48.3 Major Penalty.** **No provision** for a major under this rule.
- **48.4 Game Misconduct Penalty.** **No provision** for a game misconduct under this rule.
- **48.5 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury. If deemed appropriate, supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Most head-shot suspensions come not from the on-ice call (minor or match), but from the Department of Player Safety afterward.

### Rule 49 — Kicking

*Kicking the puck is legal except for scoring. Kicking another player is a match penalty.*

- **49.1 Kicking.** Deliberately using the skate(s) with a kicking motion to propel the puck or to contact an opponent.
- **49.2 Goals.** Kicking the puck is **permitted in all zones**, but **a goal cannot be scored by an attacking player who uses a distinct kicking motion** to propel the puck into the net with his skate/foot (including via a subsequent deflection off another player, goalkeeper or official). A goal **cannot** be scored by an attacking player who kicks any equipment (stick, glove, helmet, etc.) at the puck — including kicking the blade of his own stick — to cause the puck to cross the goal line.
  - **Deflection clarifications when a kicked puck enters the goal:**
    - (i) Off the body of any player (incl. goalkeeper) of either team = no goal.
    - (ii) Off the stick of any player (excluding goalkeeper's stick) = good goal.
    - (iii) Allowed when an attacking player kicks the puck and it deflects off his own stick then into the net.
    - (iv) Allowed when the puck enters after deflecting off an attacking player's skate, or off his skate while he is in the process of stopping.
- **49.3 Match Penalty.** **A match penalty shall be imposed on any player who kicks or attempts to kick another player.** Whether or not an injury occurs, the Referee imposes a 5-minute time penalty under this rule.
- **49.4 Fines and Suspensions.** No specified fines/suspensions; supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Skate goals are allowed; you just can't propel the puck in with a kick. The dividing line is "distinct kicking motion."

### Rule 50 — Kneeing

*Leading with the knee to make contact with an opponent.*

- **50.1 Kneeing.** A player **leading with his knee** and in some cases extending his leg outward to make contact with an opponent.
- **50.2 Minor Penalty.** Referee's discretion.
- **50.3 Major Penalty.** Referee's discretion (see 50.5).
- **50.4 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury.
- **50.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Automatic when a major has been assessed for kneeing.
- **50.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6.

**In practice:** Kneeing is a hit-from-behind cousin — the knee-on-knee collision is one of the most reliable career-ending injuries in hockey.

### Rule 51 — Roughing

*A minor altercation — punching or slamming motion that isn't worthy of a fighting major.*

- **51.1 Roughing.** Punching or slamming motion with or without the glove on the hand, normally directed at the head or face. Also applies if a player intentionally **removes an opponent's helmet** during play (Rule 9.6). A minor altercation not worthy of a major.
- **51.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for a player who strikes an opponent with hand or fist, or intentionally removes an opponent's helmet during play.
- **51.3 Match Penalty.** Match if, in the Referee's judgment, a goalkeeper uses his blocking glove to punch an opponent in an attempt to or to deliberately injure.
- **51.4 Penalty Shot.** Per Rule 57.3 — Tripping.
- **51.5 Awarded Goal.** Per Rule 25.
- **51.6 Fines and Suspensions.** No specified fines/suspensions; supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion (does not apply to helmet removal under Rule 9.6).

**In practice:** Roughing is the most common after-the-whistle minor — two players shove, push or face-wash and each gets a 2:00.

### Rule 52 — Slew-footing

*Kicking out an opponent's feet from under him while pulling backward on his upper body. Always a match penalty.*

- **52.1 Slew-footing.** Slew-footing is the act of a player using his leg or foot to knock or kick an opponent's feet out from under him, **or pushing an opponent's upper body backward** with an arm or elbow, and **at the same time** with a forward motion of his leg, knocking or kicking the opponent's feet out from under him, causing him to fall violently to the ice.
- **52.2 Match Penalty.** Any player guilty of slew-footing shall be assessed a match penalty.
- **52.3 Fines and Suspensions.** No specified fines/suspensions; supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Slew-footing is one of the most flagrant fouls; the head hitting the ice from a slew-foot is a common cause of concussion and worse.

### Rule 53 — Throwing Equipment

*Throwing a stick or any piece of equipment, with cascading consequences depending on where it happened and what it interfered with.*

- **53.1 Throwing Equipment.** A player shall not throw a stick or any other object in any zone. A player who has lost or broken his stick may only receive a stick at his own players' bench or by being handed one from a teammate on the ice (see Rule 10.3).
- **53.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for:
  - (i) A player who throws his stick or any part thereof or any other object **in the direction of the puck or an opponent in any zone**, except where covered by penalty shot (53.7) or awarded goal (53.8).
  - (ii) Defending player shoots/throws a stick or object in the defending zone **but not at** the puck or puck carrier.
  - (iii) Interference when a player moves a non-broken stick and it interferes with an opposing player (except where penalty shot or awarded goal applies) or prevents another player from retrieving a dropped stick. *Note 1:* No penalty if moving the stick doesn't interfere with the play or the opposing player isn't attempting to retrieve it. *Note 2:* No penalty when a player discards a broken portion of a stick or other object by tossing/shooting it to the side of the ice (and not over the boards) in a manner that doesn't interfere with the play or opposing player(s).
  - (iv) Minor for unsportsmanlike conduct (plus a game misconduct under 53.5) when a player throws his stick or any object or piece of equipment **outside the playing area in protest** of an official's decision.
- **53.3 Bench Minor Penalty.** Should any player/Coach/non-playing person on the bench throw anything on the ice during play or during a stoppage = bench minor for unsportsmanlike conduct (see 75.3).
- **53.4 Misconduct Penalty.** A player who **unintentionally or accidentally** throws his stick or any equipment outside the playing area.
- **53.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** A player who **intentionally** throws his stick or any equipment outside the playing area. If in protest of an official's decision: minor (53.2(iv)) + game misconduct.
- **53.6 Match Penalty.** If a player attempts to or deliberately injures by throwing a stick or other equipment.
- **53.7 Penalty Shot.** When any member of the defending team (including Coach or non-playing personnel) throws or shoots any part of a stick or any other object/equipment **at the puck or puck carrier in his defending zone**, the Referee/Linesperson allows the play to be completed and, if a goal is not scored, **awards a penalty shot** to the non-offending team (taken by the player designated by the Referee as the fouled player; if no specific victim, the Captain selects a player on the ice). If a player on a breakaway in the neutral or attacking zone is interfered with by a thrown stick/object by any member of the defending team (including a Coach/non-playing person), a penalty shot is awarded. Spectator interference on a breakaway = penalty shot to the player who was fouled.
- **53.8 Awarded Goal.** If, when the opposing goalkeeper has been removed, a member of the defending team (including Coach or non-playing person) throws or shoots any part of a stick or any equipment at the puck or puck carrier in the neutral or defending zone, **preventing a clear shot on an open net = goal awarded**.
- **53.9 Fines and Suspensions.** Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** "Throwing a stick" sounds archaic but it really does happen — usually a desperate defender stick-checking an empty-net chance from behind.

## Section 7 — Restraining Infractions (Rules 54–57)

### Rule 54 — Holding

*Restraining or impeding an opponent's progress.*

- **54.1 Holding.** Any action by a player that **restrains or impedes the progress of an opposing player whether or not he is in possession of the puck**.
- **54.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for a player who holds an opponent by using his hands, arms or legs. A player **is permitted to use his arm in a strength move, by blocking his opponent**, provided he has body position and is not using his hands in a holding manner. **A player is not permitted to hold an opponent's stick** — minor announced as "holding the stick." A player **is permitted to protect himself by defending against an opponent's stick** but must immediately release it.
- **54.3 Penalty Shot.** Per Rule 57.3.
- **54.4 Awarded Goal.** Per Rule 25.

**In practice:** "Holding the stick" is its own announcement and accounts for many seemingly-mild minors.

### Rule 55 — Hooking

*Using the stick like a hook on an opponent's body.*

- **55.1 Hooking.** Using the stick **in a manner that enables a player to restrain an opponent**. When stick-to-stick contact only, it is not hooking.
- **55.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for impeding an opponent's progress by hooking. Minor also for any player using the **shaft of the stick above the upper hand** to hold or hook an opponent.
- **55.3 Major Penalty.** Major for any player who injures an opponent by hooking (see 55.4).
- **55.4 Game Misconduct.** Mandatory when a major has been assessed for hooking causing injury.
- **55.5 Penalty Shot.** Per Rule 57.3.
- **55.6 Awarded Goal.** Per Rule 25.
- **55.7 Fines and Suspensions.** Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** A clear stick-on-glove or stick-on-body "tug" that slows the puck carrier is the typical hooking minor.

### Rule 56 — Interference

*The cornerstone of the modern game's "obstruction" enforcement: body position, possession, restraint, picks, free-hand use, and stick use.*

- **56.1 Interference.** A strict standard on acts of interference must be adhered to in all areas of the rink.
  - **Body Position:** A player skating in front of or beside his opponent, traveling in the same direction. **A player who is behind an opponent who does not have the puck may not use his stick, body or free hand in order to restrain his opponent — he must skate to gain or reestablish his position.** A player is entitled to the ice he is standing on and is not required to move; he may "block" the path of an opponent provided he is in front and moving in the same direction. **Moving laterally and without establishing body position, then making contact with the non-puck carrier, is interference.** A player is always entitled to use his body position to lengthen an opponent's path to the puck, provided his stick is not used (to make himself "bigger"), his free hand is not used, and he doesn't use the position to deliver an otherwise illegal check.
  - **Possession of the Puck:** The last player to touch the puck, other than the goalkeeper, is the player in possession. The player in possession may be checked legally provided the check is rendered **immediately following his loss of possession**.
  - **Restrain:** Actions of a player who does not have body position but uses illegal means (hook with stick, hold with hands, trip with stick, etc.) to impede an opponent not in possession. Illegal means = acts which allow a player or goalkeeper to establish, maintain or restore body position other than by skating.
  - **Pick:** A "pick" is the action of a player who **checks an opponent who is not in possession of an opponent who is unaware of the impending check/hit and therefore unprepared to defend himself**, thereby taking him out of the play. When this is done, an **interference** penalty shall be assessed.
  - **Free Hand:** When a free hand is used to **hold, pull, tug, grab or physically restrain** an opponent from moving freely = **holding**. The free hand may be used to fend off an opponent or his stick but not to hold an opponent's stick or body.
  - **Stick:** A player who does not have body position on his opponent and uses his stick (blade or shaft, incl. butt-end) to impede or prevent his opponent from moving freely = **hooking**.
- **56.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for interference shall be imposed:
  - (i) On a player who **interferes with or impedes the progress of an opponent who is not in possession of the puck**;
  - (ii) On a player who restrains an opponent attempting to **forecheck**;
  - (iii) On any player who deliberately checks an opponent (including the goalkeeper) who is not in possession;
  - (iv) On a player who shall cause an opponent not in possession of the puck to be forced **off-side**, causing a stoppage in play (if it only causes a delayed off-side, application is subject to Referee judgment);
  - (v) On a player who deliberately knocks the stick out of an opponent's hand, or prevents a player who has dropped his stick or any equipment from regaining it (see Rule 53.2(iii));
  - (vi) On a player who knocks or shoots any abandoned/broken stick or illegal puck/debris toward an opposing puck carrier in a manner that could distract;
  - (vii) On any identifiable player on the players' bench or penalty bench who, by means of his stick or body, interferes with the puck or any opponent on the ice;
  - (viii) On any player about to come onto the ice who plays the puck while one or both skates are still on the bench.
- **56.3 Bench Minor Penalty.** Bench minor when an unidentifiable player on the bench, or Coach/non-playing Club personnel, interferes (by stick or body) with the puck or any opponent.
- **56.4 Major Penalty.** Major may be imposed for severe interference (see 56.5).
- **56.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Mandatory when a major is imposed for an infraction resulting in injury.
- **56.6 Penalty Shot.** When a player in control of the puck in the neutral or attacking zone with no opponent to pass other than the goalkeeper is interfered with by a stick or any equipment thrown/shot by any member of the defending team (including Coach/non-playing personnel), a penalty shot is awarded. Coach/non-playing personnel = automatic suspension from the game, ordered to the dressing room, reported to Commissioner.
- **56.7 Awarded Goal.** When the goalkeeper has been removed and any member of his team not legally on the ice (including Coach/non-playing personnel) interferes by body, stick or equipment with the puck or an opposing player in the neutral or attacking zone, the Referee awards a goal to the non-offending team.
- **56.8 Fines and Suspensions.** Per Rule 23.6.

**In practice:** **A "pick" is the body block on a player NOT in possession; a "screen" is standing in front of the goalie without touching anyone.** Picks are penalized; screens are legal as long as no contact is made (see Rule 69 for screens in the crease).

### Rule 57 — Tripping

*Placing the stick, knee, foot, arm, hand or elbow so as to cause an opponent to trip or fall.*

- **57.1 Tripping.** A player shall not place the stick, knee, foot, arm, hand or elbow in such a manner that **causes his opponent to trip or fall**. Accidental trips which occur simultaneously with a completed play will not be penalized.
- **57.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for a player who causes an opponent to trip and fall.
- **57.3 Penalty Shot.** When a player, in the neutral or attacking zone, in control of the puck (or who could have obtained it) with no opponent to pass other than the goalkeeper, is **tripped or otherwise fouled from behind**, thus preventing a reasonable scoring opportunity = penalty shot. The Referee does not stop play until the attacking team loses possession. Conditions (all four required) — same as Rule 24.8:
  - (i) Infraction in the neutral or attacking zone.
  - (ii) Committed **from behind**.
  - (iii) Player denied a **reasonable chance to score**.
  - (iv) **No opposing player between** him and the goalkeeper.
  - **Control of the puck** = propelling the puck with the stick, hand or feet.
  - If the player makes contact with the puck first and subsequently trips the opponent: no penalty shot — just a minor.
  - If the attacking player gets around the goalkeeper with no defender between him and the open goal and is fouled from behind by the goalkeeper or another defender, **no goal awarded** (the goalkeeper is still on the ice); penalty shot awarded.
- **57.4 Awarded Goal.** When the opposing goalkeeper has been removed, a player in control of the puck in the neutral or attacking zone with no opposition is tripped or otherwise fouled, thus preventing a reasonable scoring opportunity = the Referee stops play and awards a goal.

**In practice:** Most "trips" are stick-on-skates. The penalty-shot conditions (especially "from behind" and "no defender between") are why so many breakaway fouls are *just* minors.

## Section 8 — Stick Infractions (Rules 58–62)

### Rule 58 — Butt-ending

*Using the top of the stick shaft as a weapon — always at least a double-minor.*

- **58.1 Butt-ending.** Using the **shaft of the stick above the upper hand** to check an opposing player in any manner, or jab/attempt to jab an opposing player with this part of the stick.
- **58.2 Double-minor Penalty.** A player who **attempts** to butt-end an opponent.
- **58.3 Major Penalty.** A player who **butt-ends** an opponent (see 58.5).
- **58.4 Match Penalty.** A player who **injures** an opponent as a result of a butt-end.
- **58.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Mandatory when a major is imposed for butt-ending.
- **58.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Butt-ending is far more dangerous than slashing because of the small contact point — usually penalized severely.

### Rule 59 — Cross-checking

*Forcefully checking with both hands on the stick and no part of it on the ice.*

- **59.1 Cross-checking.** Using the **shaft of the stick between the two hands** to forcefully check an opponent.
- **59.2 Minor Penalty.** Referee's discretion, based on the severity of contact.
- **59.3 Major Penalty.** Referee's discretion, based on severity (see 59.5).
- **59.4 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury.
- **59.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** **Automatic** when a major is assessed for cross-checking.
- **59.6 Penalty Shot.** Per Rule 57.3.
- **59.7 Awarded Goal.** Per Rule 25.
- **59.8 Fines and Suspensions.** Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Cross-checks near the goalmouth are the most common version — defenders try to clear attackers from the crease.

### Rule 60 — High-sticking (the player)

*Stick contact above an opponent's shoulders. Draws blood or causes injury = double-minor.*

- **60.1 High-sticking.** For "high-sticking the puck," see Rule 80. A "high stick" is one **carried above the height of the opponent's shoulders**. Players must be in control and responsible for their sticks. **Accidental contact is permitted** if the act is a normal windup or follow-through of a shooting motion, or accidental contact on the opposing center who is bent over during a face-off. A wild swing at a bouncing puck is **not** a normal windup/follow-through and any contact above shoulder height is penalized.
- **60.2 Minor Penalty.** Any contact made by a stick on an opponent above the shoulders is prohibited — minor.
- **60.3 Double-minor Penalty.** When a player carries or holds any part of his stick above the shoulders and makes contact with an opponent's neck, face or head **so that injury results, in the manner of drawing blood or otherwise** — **double-minor**. Referees may review video to confirm or rule out that the stick causing the injury was actually the stick of the penalized player. Reviews are conducted exclusively by Referee(s) on the ice in consultation with other officials using technology at ice level; Situation Room contact is for video access only.
- **60.4 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury with a high stick.
- **60.5 Penalty Shot.** Per Rule 57.3. *Note:* When a penalty shot is awarded for high-sticking that **causes an injury**, **regardless of the shot's outcome the penalized player must still serve 2 minutes of the original 4-minute penalty.**
- **60.6 Awarded Goal.** Per Rule 25.
- **60.7 Fines and Suspensions.** Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** The video-review provision exists because in fast play it can be unclear *whose* stick drew blood on a head-area scrum.

### Rule 61 — Slashing

*Forceful or powerful chop with the stick on an opponent's body, stick, or hands when it isn't an attempt to play the puck.*

- **61.1 Slashing.** Swinging the stick at an opponent, whether contact is made or not. Non-aggressive stick contact to the pant or front of the shin pads is not slashing. **Any forceful or powerful chop** with the stick on an opponent's body, an opponent's stick, or on or near the opponent's hands that, in the Referee's judgment, **is not an attempt to play the puck**, is slashing.
- **61.2 Minor Penalty.** Referee's discretion based on severity.
- **61.3 Major Penalty.** Referee's discretion based on severity. **When injury occurs, a major must be assessed** (see 61.5).
- **61.4 Match Penalty.** Referee's discretion if attempted/deliberate injury.
- **61.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** **Mandatory** when a major is assessed for slashing.
- **61.6 Penalty Shot.** Per Rule 57.3.
- **61.7 Awarded Goal.** Per Rule 25.
- **61.8 Fines and Suspensions.** Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** The "near the hands" emphasis came after a 2017 league-wide crackdown on slash-the-glove fouls that were causing finger injuries.

### Rule 62 — Spearing

*Stabbing an opponent with the point of the stick blade. Always at least a double-minor.*

- **62.1 Spearing.** **Stabbing an opponent with the point of the stick blade, whether contact is made or not.**
- **62.2 Double-minor Penalty.** A player who **spears** an opponent and **does not make contact**.
- **62.3 Major Penalty.** A player who spears an opponent (see 62.5).
- **62.4 Match Penalty.** A player who **injures** an opponent as a result of a spear.
- **62.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Mandatory when a major is assessed for spearing.
- **62.6 Fines and Suspensions.** Supplementary discipline at Commissioner's discretion.

**In practice:** Spearing is uncommon and almost always followed by supplemental discipline.

## Section 9 — Other Infractions (Rules 63–75)

### Rule 63 — Delaying the Game

*Catch-all for deliberate stoppages — puck over glass, displacing the net, falling on the puck outside the crease, freezing the puck unnecessarily, slow line changes.*

- **63.1 Delaying the Game.** A player or team may be penalized when, in the Referee's opinion, it is delaying the game in any manner.
- **63.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for delay of game shall be imposed:
  - (i) On any player (including goalkeeper) who holds, freezes or plays the puck with stick/skates/body in such a manner as to **deliberately cause a stoppage**. For the goalkeeper, this applies outside his crease.
  - (ii) On any player who deliberately **shoots or bats the puck outside the playing area** (from anywhere on the ice) during play or after a stoppage.
  - (iii) On any player who **shoots or bats the puck directly (non-deflected) out of the playing surface from his defending zone** — except where there is no glass. Determining factor is the **position of the puck when contacted**. If contact occurred while puck was inside the defending zone and it subsequently went out, minor assessed. **Puck shot over the glass behind the players' bench** = penalty. *NOTE:* When the puck is shot into the players' bench, no penalty. When the puck goes out directly off a face-off, no penalty.
  - (iv) On any player who **deliberately displaces a goal post** from its normal position. Referee stops play immediately when the offending team gains control. *NOTE:* If deliberate displacement when an opponent has an imminent scoring opportunity on an open net, **goal awarded** (63.7).
  - (v) On a player other than the goalkeeper who **deliberately falls on or gathers the puck into his body**. *NOTE:* Dropping to knees to block a shot is not a penalty if the puck goes under or becomes lodged in clothing/equipment; any use of the **hands** to make the puck unplayable is penalized.
  - (vi) On a goalkeeper who **races out of his crease in an attempt to beat the attacking player to the puck and instead jumps on it**, causing a stoppage. *NOTE:* Cutting down the angle and covering the puck after making a save is legal.
  - (vii) On a goalkeeper who, when he is in his own goal crease, deliberately drops the puck into his pads/onto the net or piles snow/obstacles, etc. (per 67.3).
  - (viii) On a goalkeeper who plays the puck outside the trapezoid behind the net (Rule 27.8).
  - (ix) For adjustments to clothing, equipment, skates or sticks. *NOTE:* No penalty when a water bottle is delivered to a goalkeeper (should be during time-outs; if used as deliberate delay, minor may be assessed).
  - (x) **No delay** permitted for repair/adjustment of goalkeeper's equipment — if adjustments are needed, the goalie leaves the ice and the substitute comes in immediately; otherwise a minor.
- **63.3 Bench Minor Penalty.** Imposed on a team that, after warning by the Referee to its Captain or Alternate Captain to place the correct number of players on the ice and commence play, fails to comply (including continually substituting goalkeepers to stall, persisting in off-side positioning, etc.).
- **63.4 Delayed Whistle.** When the net is **accidentally displaced by an attacking player** and the defending side is in control of the puck and moving out of their zone, play continues until the non-offending team loses control. Face-off then at the zone nearest where play stopped, unless that would be the non-offending team's defending zone (then face-off outside the blue line in the neutral zone). It is possible for a goal to be scored at one end while the net at the other end is dislodged — provided the team being scored upon is the team responsible for dislodging the net at the other end.
- **63.5 Objects Thrown on the Ice.** Referee stops play and the puck is faced-off at a face-off spot in the zone nearest the stoppage. When objects are thrown during a stoppage (including after a goal), the Referee has it announced over the PA that any further occurrences will result in a bench minor to the home team. Articles thrown after a **special occasion** (e.g., hat trick) **will not result in a bench minor**.
- **63.6 Penalty Shot.** No defending player (except the goalkeeper) may fall on the puck, hold it, pick it up, or gather it into his body or hands **when the puck is within the goal crease**. Violation = penalty shot to the non-offending team. The significant factor is the **location of the puck** when held/grabbed/gathered. If the puck is in the crease, penalty shot. If outside the crease and gathered by a player (other than the goalkeeper) inside the crease, minor only.
- **63.7 Awarded Goal.** When the goal post is displaced (deliberately or accidentally) by a defending player before the puck crosses the goal line, **the Referee may award a goal**. Required: post displaced by the actions of a defending player; attacking player had an imminent scoring opportunity prior to displacement; puck would have entered between the normal post positions. If the post is deliberately displaced by a goalkeeper during a breakaway, goal awarded. When the post is deliberately displaced by the defending team with their goalie pulled for an extra attacker, thereby preventing an impending goal, goal awarded. The frame is "displaced" if either or both goal pegs are no longer in their holes, or the net has come off one or both pegs, prior to or as the puck enters.
- **63.8 No Line Change.** When the goal post is accidentally displaced by a defending skater or goalkeeper causing a stoppage, the ensuing face-off is at one of the end-zone spots in the defending zone, and **the offending team is not permitted player substitutions prior to the face-off** (exceptions: replace a goalkeeper who had been substituted for an extra attacker, replace an injured player, or address a penalty that changes on-ice strength). The attacking team gets the **choice of which end-zone dot** the face-off will take place at.
- **63.9 Infractions Summary.** Includes deliberately shooting/throwing/batting the puck out of play; shooting/batting it over the glass from the defending zone; deliberately displacing the goal; refusing to place the correct number of players on the ice; persisting in off-side positioning; deliberately falling on the puck; equipment adjustments.

**In practice:** The puck-over-glass minor is the only **defensive** zone foul that doesn't require touching an opponent — it punishes desperate clearing attempts that hand back the puck. It's also the only delay-of-game call subject to Coach's Challenge.

### Rule 64 — Diving / Embellishment

*Faking a fall or injury to draw a penalty. Minor on the embellisher.*

- **64.1 Diving / Embellishment.** Any player who blatantly dives, embellishes a fall or a reaction, or who feigns an injury shall be penalized with a minor under this rule. A goalkeeper who **deliberately initiates contact with an attacking player** other than to establish position in the crease, or who otherwise acts to create the appearance of other than incidental contact, is subject to a minor for diving/embellishment.
- **64.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for attempting to draw a penalty by his actions.
- **64.3 Fines and Suspensions.** *As outlined in a League-issued memorandum distributed to all teams prior to the 2015–2016 season.* The rulebook itself does not enumerate the fine schedule; the memorandum establishes an **escalating warning-and-fines system** for both player and Coach, tracked by the Department of Player Safety. Repeat embellishers are fined publicly even when no minor was called on the ice.

**In practice:** Embellishment is publicly tracked by the Department of Player Safety so repeat offenders are fined even when no penalty was called.

### Rule 65 — Equipment

*Onus is on the player; goalkeepers' equipment cannot be repaired during play.*

- **65.1 Equipment.** Onus is on the player to maintain his equipment and uniform in playing condition as set forth in the rules.
- **65.2 Minor Penalty.** All protective equipment except gloves, headgear and goalkeepers' leg pads must be worn under the uniform. **No delay** for the repair/adjustment of a goalkeeper's equipment — adjustments require the goalkeeper to leave the ice, replaced by the substitute immediately; otherwise minor.

**In practice:** This is the rule that forces a goalie with a malfunctioning pad strap to skate to the bench rather than ask for time.

### Rule 66 — Forfeit of Game

*If a club refuses to comply with rules/resolutions/regulations affecting play, the Referee can declare a forfeit.*

- **66.1 Forfeit of Game.** In the event of failure by a Club to comply with a provision of the League constitution, by-laws, resolutions, rules or regulations affecting the playing of a game, the Referee shall — if so directed by the Commissioner or his designee — refuse to permit the game to proceed until the offending Club complies. If the Club persists in refusing, the Referee — with prior approval of the Commissioner or his designee — declares the game forfeited and the non-offending Club the winner. If both Clubs refuse to comply, the **visiting Club** is declared the winner. If forfeited before the game commences, the score is recorded **1-0** with no personal statistics credited. If forfeited mid-game, the score is recorded as zero for the loser and 1 (or such greater number that had been scored) for the winner; players retain all stats earned up to forfeit.

**In practice:** Forfeit of a regular NHL game is essentially unheard of in the modern league.

### Rule 67 — Handling Puck

*Rules covering closing a hand on the puck, concealing it, batting it on a face-off, and goalie puck-handling time limits.*

- **67.1 Handling Puck.** Penalty rules for illegal hand use on the puck. For passing the puck with the hand, see Rule 79.
- **67.2 Minor Penalty — Player.** Minor on a player who:
  - (i) **Catches it and skates with it** to avoid a check or gain a territorial advantage;
  - (ii) **Places his hand over the puck while it is on the ice in order to conceal it** from or prevent an opponent from playing it. *NOTE:* When done in his team's goal crease area, **penalty shot** (67.4) or **awarded goal** (67.5).
  - (iii) **Picks the puck up off the ice with his hand** while play is in progress.
  - Minor also for "delay of game – face-off violation" on a player taking the face-off who **attempts to win it by batting the puck with the hand**. *NOTE:* The two players in the face-off (centers) are not permitted to play the puck with their hand until a third player has at least touched it; once the face-off is complete and a winner is clear, hand-pass rules (Rule 79) apply.
- **67.3 Minor Penalty — Goalkeeper.** Delay of game minor on a goalkeeper who:
  - (i) **Holds the puck with his hands for longer than three seconds** unless actually being checked by an opponent.
  - (ii) Deliberately holds the puck in any manner causing an unnecessary stoppage.
  - (iii) Throws the puck forward toward the opponent's net. *NOTE:* If a goalie's forward throw is taken by an opponent, the Referee allows the play to be completed; if a goal is scored by the non-offending team, allowed (no penalty); if no goal, minor on the goalie.
  - (iv) Drops the puck into his pads or onto the goal net.
  - (v) Deliberately piles up snow or obstacles at or near his net.
- **67.4 Penalty Shot.** If a defending player (except the goalkeeper) falls on, holds, picks up, or gathers the puck into his body or hands from the ice **in the goal crease area** during play, **penalty shot** awarded.
- **67.5 Awarded Goal.** When a goalkeeper, prior to proceeding to his players' bench to be replaced by an extra attacker, intentionally leaves the puck in his crease where it cannot be played by an opposing player (delays the game from his crease), a goal may be awarded if the situation warrants per the rule.
- **67.6 Disallowed Goal.** **A goal cannot be scored by an attacking player who bats or directs the puck with his hand into the net.** A goal cannot be scored when an attacking player bats or directs the puck and it deflects into the net off any player, goalkeeper or official. **When the puck enters the net on a clear deflection off a glove, the goal shall be allowed.**

**In practice:** The "3 seconds" goalie-cover rule is rarely enforced literally; it's usually invoked when a goalie is freezing the puck to slow the game down with no real threat from an opponent.

### Rule 68 — Illegal Substitution

*A player entering the game improperly — too soon from the bench, before a penalty expires, or to break up a breakaway.*

- **68.1 Illegal Substitution.** Occurs when a player enters illegally from the bench (teammate not within 5' — Rule 74), from the penalty bench (penalty not yet expired), when a major is being served and the replacement does not return to the ice from the penalty bench (68.2), or when a player illegally enters for the sole purpose of preventing an opposing player from scoring on a breakaway (68.3 and 68.4). When an injured player is penalized and leaves the game, if he returns before the expiration of his penalty, he is not eligible to play (includes coincidentals where his substitute is still in the box awaiting a stoppage).
- **68.2 Bench Minor Penalty.** When a player receives a major + misconduct/game misconduct at the same time, or when an injured player receives a major and is unable to serve it himself, the team must place a substitute on the penalty bench **before the major expires**; replacement must enter from the penalty bench. Any violation = bench minor.
- **68.3 Penalty Shot.** If a player of the attacking side in possession of the puck is in such a position as to have no opposition between him and the goalkeeper, and is interfered with by a player who has illegally entered the game, **penalty shot**.
- **68.4 Awarded Goal.** When the opposing goalkeeper is removed and an attacking player attacking the unattended goal is interfered with in the neutral or attacking zone by a player who has entered the game illegally, **goal awarded** to the non-offending team.
- **68.5 Disallowed Goal.** If a penalized player returns to the ice from the penalty bench before his penalty has expired (his own error or the Timekeeper's), any goal scored by his own team while he (or his substitute) is illegally on the ice is **disallowed**; all penalties imposed on either team are served as regular penalties. The penalized player must return to serve his unexpired time (plus an additional minor if he left on his own).
- **68.6 Deliberate Illegal Substitution.** See Rule 74 — Too Many Men on the Ice.

**In practice:** This is the same provision as Rule 74's "too many men" — both rules cross-reference one another, but a deliberate illegal substitution to break up a breakaway has its own penalty-shot consequence.

### Rule 69 — Interference on the Goalkeeper

*The most-litigated rule in modern hockey. Three governing principles: contact in the crease, contact outside the crease, and how pushing/shoving changes things.*

- **69.1 Interference on the Goalkeeper.** The rule is based on the premise that **an attacking player's position, whether inside or outside the crease, should not by itself determine whether a goal should be allowed or disallowed**. Goals scored while attacking players stand in the crease may be allowed in appropriate circumstances (see 69.7). Goals **should be disallowed only if**:
  - (1) an attacking player, either by his **positioning or by contact, impairs the goalkeeper's ability to move freely within his crease or defend his goal**; or
  - (2) an attacking player initiates **intentional or deliberate contact** with a goalkeeper, **inside or outside** of his goal crease.
  - **Incidental contact** with a goalkeeper will be permitted, and resulting goals allowed, when the contact is initiated outside the goal crease, provided the attacking player has made a reasonable effort to avoid the contact. The rule is enforced by the on-ice Referees' judgment but may be subject to a Coach's Challenge (Rule 38). "Contact" includes any contact (incidental or otherwise) between a goalkeeper and attacking player(s) by stick or body. **The overriding rationale: a goalkeeper should have the ability to move freely within his goal crease without being hindered by the actions of an attacking player.** If an attacker has been pushed, shoved, or fouled by a defender so as to cause him to contact the goalkeeper, the contact will not be deemed "initiated" by the attacker provided the attacker made a reasonable effort to avoid it.
- **69.2 Penalty.** In all cases where an attacking player initiates **intentional or deliberate** contact with a goalkeeper (in or out of the crease, goal scored or not), the attacking player receives a penalty (minor or major as the Referee deems appropriate). In all cases where the infraction is to the attacking player **for hindering the goalkeeper's ability to move freely in his goal crease**, the penalty is **goaltender interference**. In exercising his judgment, the Referee should give **more significant consideration to the degree and nature of the contact** with the goalkeeper than to the exact location of the goalkeeper at the time of the contact.
- **69.3 Contact Inside the Goal Crease.** If an attacking player initiates contact (incidental or otherwise) with a goalkeeper while the goalkeeper is in his crease, and a **goal is scored**, the **goal is disallowed** (see 69.7 for the exception). If a goalkeeper, in the act of establishing position within his crease, initiates contact with an attacking player in the crease, and this results in an impairment of his ability to defend, and a goal is scored, the goal is disallowed. **If, after any contact by a goalkeeper attempting to establish position in his crease, the attacking player does not immediately vacate his current position (give ground), and a goal is scored, the goal is disallowed.** In all such cases (whether or not a goal is scored), the attacking player receives a minor for goalkeeper interference. **If an attacking player establishes a significant position within the goal crease, so as to obstruct the goalkeeper's vision and impair his ability to defend, and a goal is scored, goal disallowed.** A player "establishes a significant position within the crease" when, in the Referee's judgment, his body (or a substantial portion thereof) is within the crease **for more than an instantaneous period**.
- **69.4 Contact Outside the Goal Crease.** If an attacking player initiates **any contact, other than incidental**, with a goalkeeper while the goalkeeper is outside his crease, and a goal is scored, the **goal is disallowed**. A goalkeeper is not "fair game" outside the crease — appropriate penalty in every case of unnecessary contact, but incidental contact is permitted when the goalkeeper is playing the puck outside the crease, provided the attacker has made a reasonable effort to avoid contact.
- **69.5 Coach's Challenge.** See Rule 38.
- **69.6 Face-off Location.** Whenever the Referee stops play to **disallow a goal due to contact with the goalkeeper (incidental or otherwise)**, the resulting face-off is at the **nearest neutral zone face-off spot outside the attacking zone of the offending team**.
- **69.7 Rebounds and Loose Pucks.** In a rebound situation or where a goalkeeper and attacking player(s) are simultaneously attempting to play a loose puck (whether inside or outside the crease), **incidental contact is permitted and any resulting goal is allowed**. When a goalkeeper has been pushed into the net together with the puck by an attacking player after making a stop, **goal disallowed** (with appropriate penalty if applicable). If the attacking player was pushed/fouled by a defender, causing the goalie to be pushed into the net with the puck, the goal may be permitted. **A goal cannot be scored by pushing a player together with the puck into the goal** when the puck is under that player in or around the crease (with appropriate penalties incl. penalty shot if deemed deliberately covered).
- **69.8 Fines and Suspensions.** An attacker who initiates contact in a fashion that would otherwise warrant a penalty is assessed minor/major/game misconduct and may be subject to supplementary discipline (Rule 28).

**In practice:** The Reference Table 16 walks through every common scenario (incidental contact in crease, goalie pushed in with puck, screening, etc.) — see [Reference Tables](#reference-tables) below.

### Rule 70 — Leaving the Bench

*First player off the bench during an altercation = 10-game suspension. Second = 5 games.*

- **70.1 Leaving the Bench.** **No player may leave the players' or penalty bench at any time during an altercation or for the purpose of starting an altercation.**
- **70.2 Legal Line Change.** A player who has entered the game while play is in progress from his own bench, or legally from the penalty bench (penalty time expired), who starts an altercation may be subject to discipline under Rule 28.
- **70.3 Leaving the Players' Bench.** Players may not come on the ice during a stoppage or at the end of the first and second periods for the purpose of warming up. The Referee will report any violation to the Commissioner. Except at the end of a period or for entering legally, no player may at any time leave the players' bench. If necessary to proceed to the dressing room during the game (and required to go by way of the ice), the player must wait for a stoppage and ensure no altercations are in progress. **The player who was the first or second to leave the bench(es) during or to start an altercation = game misconduct.**
- **70.4 Leaving the Penalty Bench.** Except at the end of each period or on expiration of his penalty, no player may at any time leave the penalty bench. A player whose penalty has been served and who is to be changed must proceed at once by way of the ice and be at his bench before any change can be made (violation = bench minor for too many men). A penalized player who leaves before time expires = the Penalty Timekeeper notifies the Referees. **Any player who, having entered the penalty bench, leaves prior to his penalty expiring for the purpose of challenging an official's ruling = game misconduct + automatic 3-game suspension.** Any penalized player leaving during a stoppage and during an altercation = minor + game misconduct; the minor + unexpired time must be served by a replacement.
- **70.5 Bench Minor Penalty.** Bench minor on a team whose player(s) leave the bench for any purpose other than a change and when no altercation is in progress. A Coach or non-playing person on the ice (unless directed by official, e.g., for injury) after start of period and before end of period = bench minor + report to Commissioner.
- **70.6 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Game misconduct on the player who was the first or second to leave the bench(es) during or to start an altercation. A penalized player leaving the box during a stoppage and during an altercation = minor + game misconduct. Any player ordered to the dressing room who returns to his bench or to the ice before the appropriate time = game misconduct + automatic 10-game suspension.
- **70.7** At a stoppage following expiration of penalties, if a player exiting the penalty bench gets involved in an altercation, he is assessed the penalties he incurs **+ a game misconduct + 10-game suspension** (per 70.10). If the opponent is deemed the instigator, no game misconduct/10-game suspension.
- **70.8 Awarded Goal.** When the opposing goalkeeper is removed and an attacking player attacking the unattended goal is interfered with by a player who has entered the game illegally = goal awarded.
- **70.9 Disallowed Goal.** A penalized player returning before expiration: any goal by his own team while he is illegally on the ice = disallowed.
- **70.10 Fines and Suspensions.** **First player to leave illegally during or to start an altercation = automatic 10-game suspension. Second player = automatic 5-game suspension.** Determination by Referees in consultation with Linespersons and Off-Ice Officials; if unable to identify, referred to the Commissioner. Team fine: **$10,000 for the first instance**, increased by $5,000 for each subsequent occurrence over the next three years. Any player ordered to the dressing room who returns early = game misconduct + 10-game suspension. **Coach(es)** of involved team(s) suspended pending review + fine up to $10,000. Clubs pay pro-rata of suspended salary to the League; Clubs paying or reimbursing the player's lost salary fined $100,000. Suspensions that cannot be completed in one season carry over.

**In practice:** This is the rule designed to prevent bench-clearing brawls — it has worked effectively since adoption in the late 1980s.

### Rule 71 — Premature Substitution

*A skater jumping the gun on a goalie pull. Face-off at center if it happens past the red line; otherwise nearest spot.*

- **71.1 Premature Substitution.** When a goalkeeper leaves his goal area to be substituted by another player, the skater **cannot enter the playing surface before the goalkeeper is within 5 feet of the bench**. Premature substitution = official stops play immediately unless the non-offending team has possession (in which case the stoppage is delayed until possession changes). **No time penalty** for premature substitution. Face-off at **center ice** when play is stopped beyond the center red line; **nearest face-off spot** in the zone when stopped prior to the center red line. In all other situations not covered, a minor may result for too many men on the ice (Rule 74).
- **71.2 Announcement.** PA announces: "Play has been stopped due to premature substitution for the goalkeeper."

**In practice:** This is why teams pulling the goalie often signal the bench with a specific hand gesture — the skater must time his exit precisely.

### Rule 72 — Refusing to Play the Puck

*If a team refuses to advance the puck — to bleed clock or to avoid an obvious infraction — the play is whistled with the face-off near the offending team's net.*

- **72.1 Refusing to Play the Puck.** A team refusing to play the puck — whether to bleed clock, force a stoppage, or otherwise — is subject to penalty.
- **72.2 Hand Pass.** When a hand pass has been initiated and the receiving teammate elects not to play the puck to avoid a stoppage, and the opposing team also abstains (perhaps to let a penalty expire), the Referee stops play and orders the face-off at the nearest face-off location to where play was stopped.
- **72.3 High Stick.** Same principle when a high stick has occurred and both teams abstain from playing the puck; Referee stops play; face-off in the zone nearest where stopped (also see Rule 76.2).
- **72.4 Icing.** If the defending side intentionally abstains from pursuing the puck beyond the face-off spots on an icing, the Referee stops play and orders the face-off on the **adjacent corner face-off spot nearest the goal of the team at fault**.
- **72.5 Penalty.** When the Referee signals a delayed penalty and a player of that team intentionally abstains from playing the puck to allow time to expire on the game/penalty clocks, the Referee stops play; face-off at one of the face-off spots in the **offending team's defending zone** (see Rule 76.2).

**In practice:** This rule prevents the cheap tactic of letting the puck sit untouched on the ice during a delayed penalty.

### Rule 73 — Refusing to Start Play

*Teams that refuse to play face escalating penalties up to forfeit.*

- **73.1 Refusing to Start Play.** Applies to teams who refuse to play while both teams are on the ice, or who withdraw from the ice, or who refuse to come onto the ice at the start of the game or any period when ordered to by the Referee.
- **73.2 Procedure — Team On Ice.** **First Violation:** (i) Warn the Captain and allow 15 seconds. (ii) If still refusing, bench minor for delay of game on a player designated by the Coach. (iii) On a repeat incident, the Coach is removed from the bench and assessed a game misconduct. (iv) If still refusing, Referee declares the game forfeited to the non-offending Club. **Second Violation:** (i) Warn the Captain, allow 15 seconds. (ii) Bench minor. (iii) Notify Coach of a game misconduct. (iv) If still refusing, declare forfeit.
- **73.3 Procedure — Team Off Ice.** If a team, ordered by the Referee through the Club Executive/Manager/Coach, fails to go on the ice within **5 minutes**, the responsible official is fined $500, the game is forfeited, and the case is reported to the Commissioner.
  - (i) When apparent the team is refusing, bench minor assessed.
  - (ii) 5 minutes provided to return to the ice.
  - (iii) After 5 minutes with no return, game forfeited.
  - (iv) If team returns within the 5 minutes, bench minor assessed.
  - (v) $500 fine to the Coach/Manager/Club Executive responsible.

**In practice:** Effectively never invoked in the NHL.

### Rule 74 — Too Many Men on the Ice

*Players changing on the fly: outgoing skater must be within 5 feet of the bench before the incoming skater touches the ice.*

- **74.1 Too Many Men on the Ice.** Players may change at any time during play from the bench provided the player leaving is **within 5 feet of his bench and out of the play** before the change is made (see also Rule 71). At the discretion of the on-ice officials, if a substituting player comes on before his teammate is within the 5' limit (clearly causing too many on the ice), a bench minor may be assessed. When a retiring player is within the 5' limit and about to exit, his replacement may legally enter and the play continues without penalty. **If, during a substitution, either the entering or retiring player plays the puck, checks or makes physical contact with an opposing player while both are on the ice**, the infraction "too many men on the ice" is called. If during a substitution a player is **struck by the puck accidentally**, play is not stopped and no penalty. **During play, the player retiring must do so at the players' bench and not through any other exit** (otherwise a bench minor). A player coming onto the ice is considered on once **both skates are on the ice**; if he plays the puck or interferes with an opponent while still on the bench, penalized under Rule 56.
- **74.2 Bench Minor Penalty.** Bench minor for too many men. May be assessed by Referees or Linespersons. Should a goal be scored by the offending team prior to the whistle, the **goal is disallowed** and the bench minor assessed.
- **74.3 Penalty Bench.** A player serving a penalty who is to be changed after the penalty has been served must proceed at once by way of the ice and be within 5 feet of his bench before any change. Violation = bench minor for too many men.
- **74.4 Deliberate Illegal Substitution.** If by reason of insufficient playing time or by reason of penalties already imposed, a bench minor for deliberate illegal substitution cannot be served in its entirety within legal playing time (or anytime in overtime), **penalty shot awarded**.
- **74.5 Goalkeeper in Regular Season Overtime.** Once the goalkeeper has been removed for an extra attacker in overtime, **he must wait for the next stoppage of play before returning to his position** (he cannot change "on the fly"). Otherwise bench minor for having an ineligible player.

**In practice:** Too many men is one of the most preventable infractions in the league. It's almost always a communication failure between bench and ice.

### Rule 75 — Unsportsmanlike Conduct

*Catch-all for non-physical misconduct — abusive language toward anyone other than an official, jersey removal, sitting on the boards.*

- **75.1 Unsportsmanlike Conduct.** Players and non-playing Club personnel are responsible for their conduct at all times and must endeavor to prevent disorderly conduct before, during or after the game, on or off the ice and any place in the rink. The Referees may assess penalties for failure to do so. *NOTE:* When such conduct is directed at an official, Rule 39 applies.
- **75.2 Minor Penalty.** Minor for unsportsmanlike conduct:
  - (i) Any identifiable player who uses **obscene, profane or abusive language or gestures directed at any person**.
  - (ii) Any player guilty of unsportsmanlike conduct including but not limited to **hair-pulling, biting, grabbing hold of a face mask, etc.** If warranted (specifically when injury results), the Referee may apply Rule 21 (match).
  - (iii) Any player who **throws any object onto the ice** from the bench (or any other off-ice location).
  - (iv) Any player who **sits on the boards** (skates exposed) during play. Referee will warn the team (and so advise the other team). After one warning, minor for future violations.
  - (v) A player who **deliberately removes his jersey prior to participating in an altercation**, or who is clearly wearing a modified jersey not conforming to Rule 9 = minor for unsportsmanlike conduct **+ game misconduct**. If the altercation never materializes, **minor + 10-minute misconduct** for deliberately removing the jersey.
  - Additional unsportsmanlike penalty to a penalized player is added to unexpired time and served consecutively.
- **75.3 Bench Minor Penalty.** Bench minor for unsportsmanlike conduct:
  - (i) Player/Coach/non-playing person throws any object onto the ice from the bench (or off-ice location) during play or stoppage.
  - (ii) Unidentifiable player or any Coach/non-playing person uses obscene/profane/abusive language/gesture toward any person.
  - (iii) Coaches and/or non-playing personnel using obscene/profane/abusive language/gestures.
- **75.4 Misconduct Penalty.** Misconduct for:
  - (i) Persisting in using obscene/profane/abusive language directed at any person after being assessed a minor or bench minor under this rule.
  - (ii) Deliberately throwing any equipment out of the playing area (Referee's discretion, may rise to game misconduct).
  - (iii) Persisting in any course of conduct designed to **incite an opponent** into incurring a penalty.
  - (iv) Penalized player challenging/disputing the ruling after he has entered the penalty bench and play has resumed.
  - (v) General escalation: minor → misconduct → game misconduct.
- **75.5 Game Misconduct Penalty.** Game misconduct for:
  - (i) Persisting in conduct for which already assessed a misconduct.
  - (ii) Any player using obscene gestures on the ice or anywhere in the rink before/during/after the game. Reported to the Commissioner.
  - (iii) Coaches/non-playing personnel previously assessed a bench minor for obscene/profane language/gestures anywhere in the rink. Confidential report to the Commissioner.
  - (iv) Any player who **attempts to or deliberately injures a Manager, Coach, or other non-playing personnel**. Reported immediately and may be subject to Rule 28 sanctions.
  - (v) Player or non-playing person directing obscene/profane/abusive language/gestures to any person after the game (on or off the ice).
  - (vi) General escalation.
- **75.6 Reports.** Officials and Club officials file confidential reports on obscene gestures/language to the Commissioner.

**In practice:** Unsportsmanlike conduct overlaps significantly with Rule 39 (Abuse of Officials); the controlling question is whether the conduct was directed at an official (39) or anyone else (75).

## Section 10 — Game Flow (Rules 76–87)

### Rule 76 — Face-offs

*Every stoppage restarts with a face-off at one of the nine face-off spots. Strict procedural rules govern player positioning, encroachment, and timing.*

- **76.1 Face-off.** The action of a Referee or Linesperson dropping the puck between the sticks of two opposing players to start or resume play. A face-off begins when the official indicates the location and the officials and players take their positions; it ends when the puck has been legally dropped. **A goalkeeper may not participate in a face-off.**
- **76.2 Face-off Locations.** All face-offs are conducted on one of the **nine face-off spots**. When the play is stopped for any reason not specifically attributable to either team while the puck is in the neutral zone, the face-off is at the nearest face-off spot outside the blue line whenever possible. When unclear which of the four neutral spots is nearest, the spot giving the **home team the greatest territorial advantage** is selected.
  - **Penalties on the clock** = face-off at one of the two face-off spots in the **offending team's end zone**. **Three exceptions:**
    - (i) Penalty after a goal = center ice;
    - (ii) Penalty at the end (or start) of a period = center ice;
    - (iii) Defending team penalized and attacking players enter the attacking zone beyond the outer edge of the end-zone face-off circle = face-off in the **neutral zone** (paragraph 11).
  - The team awarded the power play has the **choice of which end-zone dot** the face-off occurs at.
  - **Two rule violations** at one stoppage (e.g., high-sticking the puck + intentional off-side): the face-off location is the one that **provides the least amount of territorial advantage to the offending team**.
  - When a goal is illegally scored as the result of a puck deflected directly off an official, the resulting face-off is at the nearest face-off spot in the zone where the puck deflected off the official.
  - Both-team infraction: face-off at the nearest face-off spot in that zone.
  - Stoppage between the end face-off spots and near the boards/corner: face-off at the spot nearest the place of stoppage in that zone.
  - **Following a stoppage, should one or both defensemen (point players) or any player coming from the bench of the attacking team enter into the attacking zone beyond the outer edge of the end-zone face-off circle** during an altercation, gathering or "scrum," the face-off takes place in the **neutral zone** near the blue line of the defending team. This also applies when an icing, intentional off-side, or penalty has been assessed and the ensuing face-off is to be in the offending team's defending zone. Should any non-offending players enter beyond the outer edge of the end-zone circle during a scrum, face-off also in the neutral zone near the defending team's blue line.
  - Goal illegally scored (directing/batting/kicking/high-sticking by the attacker) = neutral zone, nearest face-off spot.
  - Game stopped for any reason not specifically covered: face-off at the spot in the zone nearest to where the puck was last played.
  - For premature substitution (Rule 71): center ice if stopped beyond the center red line, nearest spot if before.
  - Injured player: face-off at the spot in the zone nearest the location of the puck when play was stopped (with special rules for which team controls — see Rule 8).
- **76.3 Procedure.** Once the line change procedure is complete, the Linesperson conducts the face-off. The Linesperson **must drop the puck within five (5) seconds** of his initial whistle to commence the face-off. If, during the 5 seconds, (i) a center moves prematurely, (ii) one or both centers refrain from placing the stick on the ice, (iii) any player encroaches the face-off circle, (iv) any player makes physical contact with an opponent, or (v) any player lines up in an off-side position, the **offending center(s) are replaced immediately**. In the **last 2 minutes of regulation or any time in overtime**, the Linesperson still blows the whistle to initiate the face-off, but the **5-second time limit is not enforced**; players must abide by verbal directions to allow a fast and fair face-off.
- **76.4 Procedure — Centers.** Players in the face-off stand squarely facing their opponent's end of the rink approximately **one stick length apart** with sticks on the ice. At any of the nine face-off spots, sticks must have the blade on the ice within the designated white area. At the eight face-off spots (excluding center ice), **the defending player places his stick within the designated white area first, followed immediately by the attacking player**. At center ice, the **visiting player places his stick on the ice first**. If a player fails to take his proper position immediately, the Linesperson may order him replaced. If a center is not at the designated area when 5 seconds elapsed, the Linesperson drops the puck immediately. If the center is back from the spot, "quarterbacking" or refusing to come into the face-off area when instructed, or simply slow getting to the spot when 5 seconds have elapsed, the puck is dropped. If the center attempts to arrive just as 5 seconds elapse to gain an advantage, he is removed and replaced = **face-off violation**. After an icing infraction and the center attempts to arrive just as 5 seconds elapse, **he is not removed but is warned** he has committed a face-off violation. A second violation by that team = **bench minor**. When a player is ejected from a face-off, his replacement must come quickly; the puck can be dropped without the player being set, or his team can be assessed a bench minor for delay of game on a second face-off violation. **In an icing infraction, the first face-off violation by either team will not result in the center being removed** — the Linesperson warns the team; any subsequent face-off violation by that team during the same face-off = bench minor.
- **76.5 Delaying the Game.** The two centers may not play the puck with their hand without incurring a penalty under Rule 67.2 until a third player (from either team) has at least touched the puck.
- **76.6 Procedure — Other Players.** No other player may enter the face-off circle or come within **15 feet** of the players facing-off. All players must stand on-side on all face-offs. **During end-zone face-offs, all players on the ice must position their bodies on their own side of the restraining lines marked on the outer edge of the face-off circles.** If a player other than the player taking the face-off moves into the face-off circle prior to the puck being dropped, the offending team's center is **ejected** from the face-off. **Players on the attacking team (excluding the center) must establish their position first; the defending team may then counter and hold its position until the puck is dropped.** A violation is treated as face-off encroachment and the offending center is replaced.
- **76.7 Violations.** Face-off violations:
  - (i) Encroachment by any player (other than the center) into the face-off area prior to the puck being dropped. Players on the perimeter must keep both skates outside the circle (skate contact with the line permitted). A skate crossing the line into the circle prior to the drop = face-off violation. A stick may be inside the circle if there is no physical contact with the opponent or his stick.
  - (ii) Encroachment by any player into the area between the hash marks (no physical contact with opponent or his stick).
  - (iii) Any physical contact with an opponent prior to the puck being dropped.
  - (iv) Failure by either center to properly position himself behind the restraining lines or place his stick on the ice (Rule 76.4). "Properly position" = feet on either side of the restraining lines parallel to the side boards (line contact permissible); the toe of the skate blade must not cross over the restraining lines perpendicular to the side boards as he approaches.
  - Blade of stick (at least the toe of the blade) must be placed on the ice in the designated white area and remain there until the puck is dropped.
  - **Whenever a team has committed two face-off violations during the same face-off, the Referee shall immediately assess a bench minor for delay of game.** Announced as "Bench Minor Penalty for Delay of Game – Face-off Violation."
  - Players who are late and therefore off-side for the face-off are **warned once in the game** (warning given to the Coach). Subsequent violation = bench minor for "Delay of Game – Slow Proceeding to Face-off Location."
  - No physical contact at face-offs — Referee may assess minor on the player(s) responsible; the physical contact is also a face-off violation.
- **76.8** Should the defending team not have placed enough players on the ice for the ensuing face-off, the Referee in the neutral zone notifies the offending team to place additional players on the ice. If used as a stalling tactic, warning issued and any subsequent violation = bench minor. Should the attacking team not have placed enough players, the Linesperson proceeds with the face-off in the normal manner.
- **76.9 Verification of Time.** Any loss of time on the game or penalty clocks due to a false face-off or face-off violation **must be replaced**. The Video Goal Judge may be consulted. The whistle is not blown by the official to start play; playing time commences from the instant the puck is faced-off.

**In practice:** "Face-off violations" — usually a center cheating the drop — earn most teams several warning-based bench minors per season.

### Rule 77 — Game and Intermission Timing

*Three 20-minute periods; 18-minute intermissions.*

- **77.1 Game Timing.** The time allowed for a game is **three (3) twenty-minute periods of actual play** with rest intermissions between periods.
- **77.2 Intermission Timing.** Play resumes promptly upon the expiration of **eighteen (18) minutes** (or a length designated by the League) from the completion of play in the preceding period. Timing of the intermission commences once the last official exits the ice (see Rule 34). The Game Timekeeper uses the electronic clock to display intermission time.
- **77.3 Delays.** If any unusual delay occurs within 5 minutes of the end of the first or second period, the Referee may order the next regular intermission to be taken immediately (only when requested by the home Club for delays with more than 5 minutes remaining).

**In practice:** Standard hockey clocks display 20:00 at the start of each period, counting down.

### Rule 78 — Goals

*A goal is the puck entering the net legally. The rule lists every situation in which an apparent goal is disallowed.*

- **78.1 Goals.** The team scoring the greatest number of goals during the three (3) twenty-minute periods is the winner and is credited with **two points** in the standings. If a winner is determined in overtime or shootout, the winning team gets two points and the losing team gets one point.
- **78.2 Crediting Goals.** A goal is credited to the player who propelled the puck into the opponent's goal. Each goal counts one point in his record. Only one point can be credited to any one player on a goal.
- **78.3 Crediting Assists.** When a player scores, an assist is credited to the player or players (maximum two) **who touch the puck prior to the goal scorer provided no defender plays or has control of the puck subsequently**. Each assist counts one point in the record.
- **78.4 Scoring a Goal.** A goal is scored when the puck has been **put between the goal posts by the stick of a player of the attacking side, from in front and below the crossbar, and entirely across the goal line** with the goal frame in its proper position. The goal frame is in proper position when **at least a portion of the flexible peg(s) are still inside both the goal post and the hole in the ice**. The frame could be raised somewhat on one post (or both), but as long as the flexible pegs contact the holes and the posts, the frame is not deemed displaced. A goal is scored if the puck is put into the goal in **any other manner by a player of the defending side**; the attacking player who last touched the puck is credited with the goal and assists may be awarded. **If an attacking player has the puck deflect into the net off his skate or body in any manner, the goal is allowed** (he is credited with the goal). A player who legally propels a puck into the goal crease of the opponent — and the puck becomes loose and available to another attacker — a goal scored on the play is legal.
- **78.5 Disallowed Goals.** Apparent goals are disallowed by the Referee and the appropriate announcement made:
  - (i) Puck **directed, batted or thrown into the net** by an attacker other than with a stick. If deemed deliberate, NO GOAL. **A goal cannot be scored when the puck has been deliberately batted with any part of the attacking player's body into the net.**
  - (ii) Puck **kicked using a distinct kicking motion**.
  - (iii) Puck **deflected directly into the net off an official**.
  - (iv) Goal scored while an **ineligible player** is on the ice.
  - (v) Attacker **interfered with a goalkeeper** in his goal crease.
  - (vi) Puck entered the net after making contact with an attacking player's stick **above the height of the crossbar**.
  - (vii) Video review confirms a goal at the other end of the ice on the same play.
  - (viii) Linesperson reports a double-minor for high-sticking, a major, or a match to the Referee following an attacking-team goal.
  - (ix) Goalkeeper has been pushed into the net together with the puck after making a save (see 69.7).
  - (x) Net becomes **displaced accidentally**.
  - (xi) During a delayed penalty, the offending team cannot score (the play is stopped when the offending team gains control).
  - (xii) Any goal scored other than as covered by the official rules.
- **78.6 League Initiated Review.** In the final minute of play in the 3rd period and at any point in Overtime (Regular Season and Playoffs), Hockey Operations will initiate the review of any scenario that would otherwise be subject to a Coach's Challenge.

**In practice:** The big modern category is goalie interference, which has its own dedicated Rule (69) and is the most common challenged call.

### Rule 79 — Hand Pass

*Batting the puck with the hand to a teammate is illegal outside the defending zone.*

- **79.1 Hand Pass.** A player **shall be permitted to stop or "bat" a puck in the air with his open hand, or push it along the ice with his hand**, and play shall not be stopped unless, in the opinion of the on-ice officials, he has **directed the puck to a teammate, or has allowed his team to gain an advantage**, and subsequently possession and control is obtained by a player of the offending team (directly or deflected). For "closing the hand on the puck," see Rule 67.
- **79.2 Defending Zone.** **Play will not be stopped for any hand pass by players in their own defending zone.** The location of the puck when contacted by either the player making the hand pass or the player receiving it determines the zone.
- **79.3 Face-Off Location.** When a hand pass violation occurs, the face-off shall take place at the closest face-off spot to the spot where the puck was contacted by the second player (the receiver). If the hand pass was made in the defending zone, the face-off is conducted at the defender's nearest end-zone face-off spot.

**In practice:** A hand pass in the defending zone is fine, but flick the puck to a teammate just outside your own blue line and the whistle blows.

### Rule 80 — High-sticking the Puck

*Batting the puck above the shoulders is illegal if your team retains control. Above the crossbar = disallowed goal.*

- **80.1 High-sticking the Puck.** For high-sticking infractions on the player, see Rule 60. **Batting the puck above the normal height of the shoulders with a stick is prohibited.** When a puck is struck with a high stick and subsequently comes into possession and control of a player from the offending team (including the player who made contact), either directly or deflected off any player or official, there shall be a whistle. Play is permitted to continue if:
  - (i) The puck has been batted to an opponent (Referee gives the "washout" signal; otherwise stops play).
  - (ii) A player of the defending side bats the puck into his own goal — goal is allowed.
  - **Cradling the puck on the blade of the stick (lacrosse-style) above the shoulders is prohibited** and play is stopped. If done on a penalty shot or shootout attempt, the shot is stopped immediately and considered complete.
- **80.2 Face-Off Location.** When play is stopped for high-sticking the puck, the ensuing face-off takes place at the spot that **provides the least amount of territorial advantage to the team striking the puck**, either where the puck was contacted illegally or where it was last played by the offending team. If the attacking team is at fault and play is stopped in the attacking zone, the face-off must move to the nearest face-off spot in the neutral zone.
- **80.3 Disallowed Goal.** When an attacking player causes the puck to enter the goal by contacting it **above the height of the crossbar**, either directly or deflected off any player or official, the goal is not allowed. **The determining factor is where the puck makes contact with the stick.** If contact is at or below the level of the crossbar, the goal is allowed. A goal scored as a result of a defending player striking the puck with his stick above the crossbar into his own goal **shall be allowed**.

**In practice:** Replay reviews focus tightly on the **point of contact between stick and puck** relative to a horizontal line at the crossbar height — about 4 feet.

### Rule 81 — Icing

*Hybrid icing: the linesperson decides which player would touch the puck first based on who reaches the face-off dot first. Icing forbids the offending team from changing lines.*

- **81.1 Icing.** The center red line divides the rink into two halves. **For purposes of this rule, the point of last contact with the puck by the team in possession is used to determine whether icing has occurred.** The team in possession must "**gain the line**" in order to nullify a potential icing. "Gaining the line" = the puck, **while on the player's stick (not the player's skate)**, must make contact with the center red line in order to nullify a potential icing.
  - There are **two judgments**: first the Linesperson determines whether the puck will cross the goal line; then, once determined that it will, **icing is completed upon the determination of which player (attacking or defending) would first touch the puck**. This decision is made **by no later than the instant the first player reaches the end-zone face-off dots, with the player's skate being the determining factor**. (Hybrid icing.)
  - Should the puck travel around the boards and/or back toward the end-zone face-off dots, the same procedure applies — Linesperson determines within a similar distance who will touch the puck first.
  - **The determining factor is which player would first touch the puck, not which player would first reach the end-zone face-off dots.**
  - **If the race for the puck is too close to determine** by the time the first player reaches the end-zone face-off dots, **icing shall be called**.
  - The puck striking or deflecting off an official does not automatically nullify a potential icing.
- **81.2 Face-Off Location.** Following an icing, the attacking team has the **choice of which end-zone dot** the face-off will take place at. If the defending side **intentionally abstains from playing the puck promptly** when in a position to do so, the Referee stops play and orders the face-off on the **adjacent corner face-off spot nearest the goal of the team at fault**. If the Linesperson erred in calling icing (regardless of whether either team is short-handed), the puck is faced off on the **center ice face-off spot**.
- **81.3 Goalkeeper.** If, in the opinion of the Linesperson, the goalkeeper **feigns playing the puck, attempts to play the puck, or skates in the direction of the puck on an icing at any time**, the potential icing is not called and play continues. However, if a goalkeeper is legitimately out of the crease in an attempt to go to the bench to be substituted for an extra attacker and in no way attempts to play the puck, the icing should not be nullified. If the goalkeeper is out of his crease prior to the shot being taken and simply retreats to his crease making no attempt to play the puck or feign playing it, the potential icing remains in effect.
- **81.4 Line Change on Icing.** **A team that is in violation of this rule shall not be permitted to make any player substitutions prior to the ensuing face-off.** Exceptions: replace a goalkeeper who had been substituted for an extra attacker; replace an injured player; or when a penalty is assessed that affects on-ice strength. The determination of players on ice is made **when the puck leaves the offending player's stick**.
- **81.5 No Icing.** When the puck is shot and rebounds from the body or stick of an opponent in his own half of the ice so as to cross the goal line of the player shooting, it is **not icing**. When a puck shot from the team's own half deflects several times before crossing the center red line, icing is nullified if **at least one of these deflections was off an opposing player**. If the puck goes beyond the goal line in the opposite half directly from either player while facing-off, **not icing**. If, in the Linesperson's opinion, **any player (other than the goalkeeper) of the opposing team is able to play the puck before it passes his goal line but has not done so, play continues** and the icing violation is not called (including when the opposing team is making player substitutions and chooses not to play it to avoid too-many-men). **If the puck touches any part of a player of the opposing side**, including his skates or his stick, or **the opposing goalkeeper** (incl. skates or stick), **at any time before or after crossing the goal line, it is not icing.** If a goalkeeper takes any action to dislodge the puck from the back of the net, icing is not called.
- **81.6 Numerical Strength.** **If the puck was shot by a player of a side below the numerical strength of the opposing team, play continues and the icing violation is not called.** When a team is short-handed and a penalty is about to expire, the decision on icing is determined at the **instant the penalty expires**. Should the puck be released from the stick of the player shooting prior to the penalty expiring, the icing infraction does not apply (the action of the penalized player remaining in the box does not alter the ruling). When a team is short-handed by a major and a goal is scored by the non-offending team, the player serving the major remains on the bench; the released penalized player's stick contact remains tied to numerical strength.

**In practice:** Hybrid icing was adopted to prevent injuries from collisions during the old "touch icing" races to the puck — now the linesperson stops play before the race ends if it's clear the defender would have won.

### Rule 82 — Line Changes

*After every stoppage: visitor changes first (5 sec), home changes second (8 sec). After an icing the offending team can't change.*

- **82.1 Line Change.** Following a stoppage, the **visiting team places a lineup on the ice ready for play first** and no substitution may be made from that time until play has resumed. The **home team may then make any desired substitution, except in cases following an icing**, which does not result in delay of the game. "Placing a lineup on the ice" = both teams place the full complement of players (and not exceed) within the line-change time frame. If there is any undue delay, the Referee orders the offending team(s) to take positions immediately and permits no further changes. Once the line change procedure has been completed (82.2), **no additional player substitutions are permitted until the face-off has been completed legally and play has resumed**, except when a penalty is imposed that affects on-ice strength of either team. **A team in violation of Rule 63.8 (delaying the game — accidental net displacement) or Rule 81 (icing) shall not be permitted to make any player substitutions prior to the ensuing face-off.** Exceptions: replace a goalkeeper substituted for an extra attacker; replace an injured player; or to address a penalty affecting on-ice strength.
- **82.2 Procedure.** Following a stoppage, the Referee enforces:
  - (i) **5 seconds** for the visiting team to make its line change.
  - (ii) Referee raises hand to indicate no further changes by the visitor and to commence the home team's change.
  - (iii) **8 seconds** for the home team to make its line change.
  - (iv) Referee lowers his hand to indicate no further home changes.
  - (v) Any attempt by either team to change after the Referee's signal, attempt too many men on the ice for the subsequent line change, or make additional personnel changes, is not permitted — Referee sends them back and issues a warning. Subsequent violations during the rest of the game (including overtime) = bench minor for delaying the game ("Bench Minor Penalty for Delay of Game – Improper Line Change").
  - (vi) Linesperson conducting the face-off will blow his whistle (once) to begin the 5-second face-off timing.
  - (vii) Players slow to the face-off location (after the 5-second warning whistle) or in off-side position for the ensuing face-off are warned once in the game; warning given to Coach. Subsequent violation = bench minor.
  - (viii) **In the last 2 minutes of regulation and any time in OT, (vi)–(vii) above are not applicable** — Linesperson gives teams a reasonable amount of time after (1)–(5) are enforced.
- **82.3 Bench Minor Penalty.** Any attempt by the visiting team to make a change after the Referee's signal = bench minor for delay of game. Players must proceed directly to the location of the face-off; any stalling = bench minor for delay of game ("Slow Proceeding to Face-off Location" or "Slow Proceeding to Players' Bench"). A player wishing to retire during play must do so at the players' bench and not through any other exit; otherwise bench minor.

**In practice:** The 5-and-8-second rule ensures home-ice advantage for line matching: visiting team changes first; home coach reacts.

### Rule 83 — Off-side

*Attacking players cannot precede the puck across the defending team's blue line. Delayed off-sides clear when attackers vacate the zone.*

- **83.1 Off-side.** **A player is on-side when either of his skates are in contact with the blue line, or on his own side of the line, at the instant the puck completely crosses the leading edge of the blue line.** "On his own side of the line" is defined by a **"plane"** of the blue line, extending from the leading edge upward. If a player's skate has yet to break the plane prior to the puck completely crossing the leading edge, he is on-side. A player **controlling the puck** who shall cross the line ahead of the puck is **not off-side** provided he had **possession and control prior to** his skates crossing the leading edge. A player legally carrying, passing or playing the puck back into his own defending zone while an opposing player is in such defending zone: off-side is ignored and play continues.
- **83.2 Deflections / Rebounds.** When a defending player propels the puck out of his defending zone and the puck **clearly rebounds off a defending player in the neutral zone back into the defending zone**, all attacking players are eligible to play the puck. However, **any action by an attacking player that causes a deflection/rebound off a defending player in the neutral zone back into the defending zone** (stick check, body check, physical contact) = delayed off-side. A puck deflecting back into the defending zone off an **official in the neutral zone** = off-side or delayed off-side as appropriate. A puck that deflects off an attacking player **outside the blue line** into the attacking zone, regardless of who originally propelled it, = off-side or delayed off-side. A puck propelled by the attacking team and deflecting off any player = off-side or delayed off-side.
- **83.3 Delayed Off-side.** A situation where an attacking player (or players) has preceded the puck across the attacking blue line, but **the defending team is in a position to bring the puck back out of its defending zone without any delay or contact with an attacking player, or the attacking players are in the process of clearing the attacking zone**. If, during a delayed off-side, an attacking player in the attacking zone elects to proceed to his players' bench (which extends into the attacking zone) to be replaced, he is considered to have cleared the zone when both skates are off the ice. If his replacement comes onto the ice in the attacking zone while the delayed off-side is still in effect, he too must clear the attacking zone. Once **all** attackers have cleared, the Linesperson lowers his arm and attackers may legally enter and pursue the puck. The Linesperson drops his arm to nullify the off-side and allow play to continue if:
  - (i) All players of the offending team clear the zone at the same instant (skate contact with the blue line) permitting attacking players to re-enter, **or**
  - (ii) The defending team passes or carries the puck into the neutral zone.
- **83.4 Disallowed Goal.** If the puck is shot into the attacking zone creating a delayed off-side, play is allowed to continue under normal clearing-the-zone rules. Should the puck (as a result of this shot) enter the defending team's goal, either directly or off the goalkeeper, a player, the boards, the glass, a piece of equipment or an official, **the goal is disallowed** as the original shot was off-side. The fact that the attacking team may have cleared the zone prior to the puck entering the goal has no bearing. Face-off conducted at the spot in the zone closest to the point of origin of the shot that gives the offending team the least amount of territorial advantage. **The only way an attacking team can score on a delayed off-side situation is if the defending team shoots or puts the puck into their own net without action or contact by the offending team.** Other than via a successful Coach's Challenge (Rule 38), no goal can be disallowed after the fact for an off-side violation, except for the human factor involved in blowing the whistle.
- **83.5 Coach's Challenge.** See Rule 38.
- **83.6 Face-Off Location.** Off-side: face-off in the neutral zone at the face-off spot nearest the attacking zone of the offending team when the violation occurs as a result of carrying the puck over, or at the face-off spot in the zone closest to the point of origin of the shot or pass (even if deflected). For every delayed off-side situation, including intentional off-side, the Linesperson raises his non-whistle arm. He allows play to continue and, if a stoppage occurs, **four possible face-off locations**:
  - (i) Carried over the blue line = face-off outside the blue line.
  - (ii) Shot into the zone (or errant pass) = nearest face-off spot in the zone from where the puck was shot or where it last deflected.
  - (iii) Delayed off-side and a defending player shoots the puck over the glass and out of play = face-off in the defending zone end-zone spot (the defender gets a minor for delay of game).
  - (iv) Delayed off-side and the original shot deflects off a defending player and out of play = face-off at the nearest face-off spot in the zone from where the puck was shot.
  - When the defending team is about to be penalized and the Linesperson has a delayed off-side signaled against the attacking team on the same play, face-off conducted at one of the defending zone end-zone spots.
- **83.7 Intentional Off-side.** An intentional off-side is one made for the purpose of securing a stoppage of play regardless of the reason (whether either team is short-handed). If the Linesperson judges intentional, **face-off at the end face-off spot in the defending zone of the offending team**.

**In practice:** The Coach's Challenge for off-side now triggers reviews where a player's skate left the ice by a millimeter while crossing the blue line — frame-by-frame analysis matters.

### Rule 84 — Overtime

*Regular season: 5 minutes 3-on-3, then shootout. Playoffs: full 20-minute periods, 5-on-5, sudden death.*

- **84.1 Overtime — Regular Season.** If the score is tied after three (3) twenty-minute periods, each team is awarded **one point** in the standings. Teams then play an additional overtime period of **not more than 5 minutes** with the team scoring first declared the winner and being awarded an additional point. **The overtime period shall be played with each team at a numerical strength of three (3) skaters and one (1) goalkeeper.** Overtime commences following a shoveling of the entire ice surface using the same procedures as during TV time-outs. Goalkeepers must go to their respective benches during the rest period; penalized players must remain on the penalty bench. The players remain at their respective benches during the shoveling. **Teams are not permitted to return to the dressing room** during this time. **Teams must change ends** for the overtime period. (For the entire overtime period, teams defend the same end-zone as they do in the second period.) Should a penalized player exit the penalty bench, he shall be returned immediately by the officials with no additional penalty being assessed, unless he commits an infraction.
- **84.2 Overtime — Regular Season — Extra Attacker.** A team is allowed to pull its goalkeeper in favor of an additional skater in overtime. **However, should that team lose the game during the time the goalkeeper has been removed, it would forfeit the automatic point** gained in the tie at the end of regulation, *except* if the goalkeeper has been removed at the call of a delayed penalty against the other team. Should the goalkeeper proceed to his bench for an extra attacker due to a delayed penalty call, and the non-offending team shoot the puck directly into its own goal, the game is over and the team to be penalized is declared the winner. Once the goalkeeper has been removed for an extra attacker in regular-season overtime, **he must wait for the next stoppage of play before returning to his position** (he cannot change "on the fly"); otherwise bench minor for having an ineligible player.
- **84.3 Overtime — Regular Season — Penalties.** When regulation ends and teams are **5-on-3**, overtime starts 5-on-3. Once player strength reaches 5-on-4 or 5-on-5, at the next stoppage strength is adjusted to **4-on-3 or 3-on-3** as appropriate. When regulation ends and teams are **4-on-4**, overtime starts **3-on-3**. If regulation ends 3-on-3, overtime starts 3-on-3; once strength reaches 4-on-4, 5-on-4 or 5-on-5, at the next stoppage strength is adjusted to 3-on-3 or 4-on-3 as appropriate. **At no time will a team have less than three players on the ice** — this may require a fourth and/or fifth skater to be added in the event penalties are assessed. (See Tables 17 and 18.) If a team is penalized in overtime, teams play **4-on-3**. If both teams are penalized with minor penalties at the same stoppage (no other penalties in effect), teams continue 3-on-3. In overtime, if a team is penalized such that a two-man advantage is created, the team being penalized adds a player so the strength is 5-on-3 (effectively); at the first stoppage after the two-man advantage is no longer in effect, the numerical strength reverts to 4-on-3 or 3-on-3 as appropriate.
- **84.4 Shootout.** If the game remains tied at the end of the 5-minute overtime period, teams proceed to a shootout. Rules governing the shootout are the same as those under Rule 24 — Penalty Shot. The shootout commences following a shoveling of the entire ice surface. Goalkeepers occupy the goal closest to their team's bench. The **home team has the choice of shooting first or second**. Teams alternate shots. (For the shootout, teams defend the same end-zone as in the first and third periods.) Eligible players from each team shall participate; they proceed in the order the Coach selects. **All players are eligible unless serving a 10-minute misconduct or assessed a game misconduct or match penalty.** When a goalkeeper has been assessed a misconduct, the player designated to serve it becomes ineligible for the shootout. If the misconduct is assessed during the course of the shootout, the player designated to serve it must be one who has yet to shoot and thus becomes ineligible. Guidelines for stick measurements: Rule 10.7. Once the shootout begins, the goalkeeper cannot be replaced unless he is injured. **No warm-up for a substitute goalkeeper.** Each team gets three shots unless the outcome is determined earlier. After each team has taken three shots, if the score remains tied, the shootout proceeds to "sudden-death" format. **No player may shoot twice until everyone eligible has shot.** If, because of injury or penalty, one team has fewer players eligible than its opponent, both teams may select from players who have already shot — this continues until the smaller team has again used all eligible shooters. Regardless of the number of shootout goals, the final official score is the score at the end of overtime + 1 (for the team winning the shootout). The losing goalkeeper is not charged with the extra goal against. The player scoring the game-winning goal in the shootout is not credited with a goal in his personal stats. If a team declines to participate, the team is deemed to have lost.
- **84.5 Overtime — Playoffs.** In the Stanley Cup Playoffs, when a game is tied after three (3) twenty-minute regular periods of play, the teams take a 15-minute intermission and resume playing **twenty (20) minute periods**, changing ends for the start of each overtime period. **The team scoring the first goal in overtime is declared the winner of the game.** Playoff OT is **5-on-5** sudden death; no shootout.

**In practice:** 3-on-3 OT creates wide-open ice and famously high goal rates per 60 minutes. Playoff OT is the inverse — closed gaps, no risk-taking, and games sometimes lasting deep into the night.

### Rule 85 — Puck Out of Bounds

*If the puck leaves the playing surface, face-off is at the nearest spot from where it was shot or deflected out.*

- **85.1 Puck Out of Bounds.** When a puck goes outside the playing area at either end or either side of the rink, strikes any obstacles above the playing surface other than boards or glass, or causes the glass, lighting, timing device or supports to break, it is **faced off at the nearest face-off spot in the zone from where it was shot or deflected out of play**. Should officials rule the shot or deflection originated from the neutral or defending zones, the resulting face-off location shall be the nearest face-off spot closest to the origin of the shot or deflection that gives the offending team the **least amount of territorial advantage**.
- **85.2** If the puck comes to rest on top of the boards, it is considered to be in play and may be played legally by hand or stick. When the puck goes outside the playing area directly off the face-off, regardless of which player may have last contacted it, the face-off remains in the same spot and no penalty is assessed. When the puck is shot and makes contact with the gloves or body of a player hanging over the players' bench, or enters the bench through an open bench door, the face-off is at the nearest face-off spot in the zone from where the puck was shot. If the puck hits an opposing player's glove/body hanging over the opposing team's bench, or enters that bench through an open door, face-off in the neutral zone adjacent to the opponent's bench. Should the puck strike the curved glass located at the end of either players' bench, play is stopped when observed by any on-ice official; face-off determined as if the puck went outside the playing area. Should the puck strike the spectator netting at the ends and the side of the rink, play is stopped (unless the on-ice officials missed it and play continues). **The puck may be played off the goal netting by either team.** However, should the puck remain on the goal netting for more than **3 seconds**, play is stopped. Should the goalkeeper use his stick or glove to freeze the puck on the back of the net, or should a defending player shield an attacking player from playing the puck off the back of the net, the face-off shall take place at one of the face-off spots in the defending zone. Should the puck go under the goal either from behind or the side (or through the mesh), if witnessed by an on-ice official, play is stopped immediately; face-off at the nearest face-off spot in the zone nearest the location of the stoppage.
- **85.3 Puck Out of Sight.** Should a scramble take place or a player accidentally fall on the puck and the puck be out of sight of the Referee, he shall immediately blow his whistle. The puck is faced-off at the nearest face-off spot in the zone where the play was stopped unless otherwise provided.
- **85.4 Puck Striking Official.** Play is not stopped if the puck touches an official anywhere on the rink, regardless of whether a team is short-handed or not. A puck that deflects back into the defending zone off an official who is in the neutral zone is deemed off-side per Rule 83. The puck striking or deflecting off an official does not automatically nullify a potential icing. When a puck deflects off an official and goes out of play, the ensuing face-off is at the face-off spot in the zone nearest to where the puck deflected off the official. **If a goal is scored as a result of being deflected directly into the net off an official, the goal is not allowed.**
- **85.5 Face-Off Location.** Should any player cause the puck to go out of play or become unplayable in any zone, the face-off is at the spot in the zone from which the puck was shot. If deflected out of play, at the nearest face-off spot in the zone where it deflected out. If the zone is the neutral zone, the spot selected gives the offending team the **least amount of territorial advantage**. For a puck that is unplayable due to being lodged in the netting or being frozen between opposing players, face-off at either of the adjacent face-off spots or at the nearest face-off spot in the zone from which the puck was shot. If the attacking team shoots the puck into the zone and a delayed off-side is indicated, or if the attacking team commits a game-flow infraction such as contacting the puck with a high stick or batting it with a glove (causing a stoppage), the ensuing face-off is in the neutral zone outside the offending team's attacking zone.
- **85.6 Minor Penalty.** Minor for delay of game on a goalkeeper who **deliberately drops the puck on the goal netting** to cause a stoppage (Rule 67.3).
- **85.7 Verification of Time.** Any loss of time on the game or penalty clocks due to the puck going out of play must be replaced. The Video Goal Judge may be consulted.

**In practice:** This rule plus Rule 63.2(iii) is the legal basis for the puck-over-glass-from-defending-zone minor — the puck going into the netting itself is *not* the penalty (the deliberate-clear nature is).

### Rule 86 — Start of Game and Periods

*Center-ice face-off opens every period. Home club chooses ends to defend.*

- **86.1 Start of Game and Periods.** The game commences at the time scheduled by a **face-off in the center of the rink** and is renewed promptly at the conclusion of each intermission in the same manner.
- **86.2 Bench Minor Penalty.** Bench minor for delay of game on either or both teams if:
  - (i) They are not on the ice or proceeding to the ice to start the second, third or any overtime period when the intermission time on the clock has expired.
  - (ii) At the start of the second, third and any overtime period, **only starting players proceed directly to their respective benches** — skating, warm-ups, or on-ice activities by non-starters is not permitted.
  - (iii) When the visiting team must proceed by way of the ice to their dressing room at the end of a period, they must wait for a signal from one of the officials before proceeding.
- **86.3 Choice of Ends.** **The home club has the choice of goals to defend at the start of the game**, except where both benches are on the same side of the rink (then the home club starts the game defending the goal nearest its own bench). Teams change ends for each period of regulation time, and in playoffs for each overtime period (Rule 84).
- **86.4 Delays.** No delay permitted by reason of any ceremony, exhibition, demonstration or presentation unless consented to reasonably in advance by the visiting team.
- **86.5 End of Periods.** At the end of each period, the home team players proceed directly to their dressing room while the visiting team players wait for a signal from the official before proceeding (if going via the ice). Failure = bench minor for delay of game.
- **86.6** Twenty (20) minutes before the time scheduled for the start of the game, both teams vacate the ice and proceed to their dressing rooms while the ice is being flooded. Both teams shall be signaled by the Game Timekeeper to return to the ice together in time for the scheduled start.
- **86.7 Start of Game.** At the beginning of the game, if a team fails to appear on the ice promptly without proper justification, a fine is assessed against the offending team; the amount is decided by the Commissioner.
- **86.8 Start of Periods.** At the beginning of the second and third periods (and overtime periods in playoffs), clubs must be on the ice or be observed proceeding to the ice **at 0:00 on the intermission clock**. Failure = bench minor for delay of game. Teams proceed directly to their respective benches; only the starting lineup is allowed on the ice. **The visiting team immediately places its starting lineup at the face-off circle; the home team follows, with the Referee allowing the home team to make a line change if so desired prior to the face-off.** Skating, warm-ups or on-ice activities by non-starters = bench minor for delay of game. **No warm-up involving pucks on the ice for a goalkeeper at the start of any period.** After one warning, continued = delay of game minor.

**In practice:** Teams change ends after each period so neither side has the advantage of the long-change for two periods in a row.

### Rule 87 — Time-outs

*Each team gets one 30-second time-out per game.*

- **87.1 Time-out.** Each team is permitted to take **one 30-second time-out**, which must be taken during a normal stoppage of play. Only one team is permitted to use its time-out during any one stoppage. **No time-out shall be granted to the defensive team following an icing, following the goalkeeper causing a stoppage of play from a shoot-in from beyond the center red line, or when a defending player accidentally dislodges the net** causing a stoppage. When a penalty shot has been awarded, no time-out will be granted once instructions have been given to the player taking the shot and the goalkeeper defending the shot. **No time-out will be granted during the shootout.** No warm-up involving pucks on the ice for a goalkeeper or replacement goalkeeper during a time-out (after one warning, delay of game minor).
- **87.2 TV Time-outs.** Television commercial time-out guidelines are established by the League. **No commercial time-out is permitted after the scoring of a goal.** No commercial time-out after the calling of an icing infraction, following the goalkeeper causing a stoppage from a shoot-in from beyond the center red line, or when a defending player accidentally dislodges the net — except when penalties affecting on-ice strength are assessed.

**In practice:** Most teams save their 30-second time-out for the end of a close game — to set up a key offensive faceoff or rest pulled-goalie skaters.

## Glossary

**Aggressor.** A player in a fight who continues throwing punches at a defenseless or unwilling opponent. Major + game misconduct. (Rule 46.2.)

**Assist.** Credit awarded to the player(s) — maximum two — who touched the puck immediately prior to the goal scorer, provided no defender played or controlled the puck in between. (Rule 78.3.)

**Attacking Zone.** The zone farthest from the team's own goal. (Rule 1.6.)

**Bench Minor.** A 2-minute penalty served by a designated player chosen by the Coach. (Rule 17.)

**Blue Line.** A 12"-wide blue line drawn 64 ft out from each goal line. Defines the boundary of the attacking and defending zones. (Rule 1.5.)

**Boarding.** Checking a defenseless opponent so he hits or impacts the boards violently or dangerously. (Rule 41.)

**Breakaway.** A player in control of the puck with no defender between him and the goalkeeper.

**Butt-ending.** Using the top of the shaft (above the upper hand) to check or jab an opponent. (Rule 58.)

**Captain.** The team's only player permitted to discuss rule interpretations with the Referee. Wears a "C." (Rule 6.)

**Center Line / Center Red Line.** A 12"-wide red line dividing the rink at the midpoint. (Rule 1.5.)

**Charging.** A check delivered as the result of distance traveled — violently checking an opponent. (Rule 42.)

**Clipping.** Throwing the body across or below the knees of an opponent from any direction. (Rule 44.)

**Coincidental Penalties.** Equal-and-opposite penalties to both teams that cancel each other out so far as on-ice strength is concerned. (Rule 19.)

**Cross-checking.** Using the stick (between the two hands, with no part of it on the ice) to forcefully check an opponent. (Rule 59.)

**Defending Zone.** The zone containing the team's own goal. (Rule 1.6.)

**Delayed Off-Side.** Linesperson raises non-whistle arm; play continues until all attackers clear the zone or the defending team passes/carries the puck out. (Rule 83.3.)

**Delayed Penalty.** The arm-up signal indicating a penalty will be imposed once the offending team gains possession of the puck. (Rule 15.1.)

**Distinct Kicking Motion.** A deliberate motion to propel the puck with the foot or skate. Renders any resulting goal NO GOAL. (Rule 49.2 / 37.4.)

**Double-minor.** A 4-minute penalty (one penalized player). (Rule 18.)

**Elbowing.** Using the elbow to illegally check an opponent. (Rule 45.)

**Embellishment.** Faking or exaggerating a fall or reaction to draw a penalty. (Rule 64.)

**Face-off.** The action of an official dropping the puck between two opposing players to start or resume play. (Rule 76.)

**Forechecking.** Attempting to regain possession of the puck in the opponent's zone.

**Game Misconduct.** Ejection from the game; substitute permitted; 10 minutes added to player's records. (Rule 23.)

**Goal Crease.** A 6'-radius semicircle in front of each goal painted light blue. The protected area for the goalkeeper. (Rule 1.7.)

**Goal Line.** The 2"-wide red line drawn across the ice at each end, 11 ft from the end boards. (Rule 1.5.)

**Hand Pass.** Stopping or batting the puck in the air with an open hand or pushing it along the ice with the hand. Illegal outside the defending zone. (Rule 79.)

**Head-butting.** Using the head as a weapon. (Rule 47.)

**High-stick.** A stick carried above the height of the opponent's shoulders. (Rule 60.) (See Rule 80 for high-sticking the puck.)

**Holding.** Restraining or impeding the progress of an opposing player. (Rule 54.)

**Hooking.** Using the stick in a manner that restrains an opponent. (Rule 55.)

**Hybrid Icing.** The current method of judging icing — determined by whether the defender would touch the puck first, by reference to who reaches the end-zone face-off dot first. (Rule 81.1.)

**Icing.** A team not short-handed shoots the puck from behind the center red line across the opposing goal line; a defender would have touched the puck first. (Rule 81.)

**Illegal Check to the Head.** A check to an opponent where contact with the head was avoidable, judged by three factors: whether the player hit squarely through the body, whether the opponent put himself in a vulnerable posture, and whether the opponent materially changed body/head position immediately before contact. (Rule 48.)

**Instigator.** Player who initiates a fight by actions or demeanor. Instigating minor + major + 10-min misconduct. (Rule 46.11.)

**Interference.** Restraining or impeding an opponent not in possession of the puck, or hitting an opponent who is unaware of the impending check. (Rule 56.)

**Kicking.** Deliberately using the skate(s) with a kicking motion to propel the puck or contact an opponent. (Rule 49.)

**Kneeing.** Leading with the knee to make contact with an opponent. (Rule 50.)

**Linesperson.** On-ice official primarily responsible for off-side and icing calls. Two per game.

**Major Penalty.** A 5-minute penalty; full time served even on a goal. (Rule 20.)

**Match Penalty.** Ejection + mandatory league review; player suspended pending review. (Rule 21.)

**Minor Penalty.** A 2-minute penalty; ends early if the opponent scores. (Rule 16.)

**Misconduct.** A 10-minute penalty served by the player; team is not short-handed. (Rule 22.)

**Neutral Zone.** The central portion of the rink between the two blue lines. (Rule 1.6.)

**Off-Side.** An attacking player precedes the puck into the attacking zone. (Rule 83.)

**Pick.** A body block by a player on an opponent who is not in possession of the puck and is unaware of the impending check. Always interference. (Rule 56.1.)

**Power Play.** A team playing with a numerical advantage due to a penalty against the opposition.

**Premature Substitution.** A skater taking the ice before the goalkeeper is within 5 feet of the bench. (Rule 71.)

**Puck.** Vulcanized rubber disc, 1" thick, 3" diameter, 5½–6 oz., frozen before use. (Rule 13.)

**Referee.** Senior on-ice official; calls penalties and rules on disputed goals. Two per game.

**Referee's Crease.** A 10'-radius semicircle in red, 2" wide, on the ice in front of the Penalty Timekeeper's seat. (Rule 1.7.)

**Roughing.** Punching or slamming motion not severe enough to warrant a major. (Rule 51.)

**Screen.** Standing in front of the goalkeeper to obstruct vision without making contact. Legal outside the crease.

**Shootout.** Tiebreaker for regular-season games — alternating penalty shots, three rounds minimum, then sudden death. (Rule 84.4.)

**Short-handed.** A team below the numerical strength of its opponent on the ice. (Rule 16.2.)

**Slashing.** Swinging the stick at an opponent (with or without contact) or a forceful chop on the body/stick/hands that is not an attempt to play the puck. (Rule 61.)

**Slew-footing.** Kicking out an opponent's feet from under him while pulling backward on his upper body. Match penalty. (Rule 52.)

**Spearing.** Stabbing an opponent with the point of the stick blade. (Rule 62.)

**Third Man In.** First player to intervene in an altercation already in progress. Game misconduct. (Rule 46.16.)

**Time-out.** Each team gets one 30-second time-out per game. (Rule 87.)

**Too Many Men.** Bench minor when a team has 7+ skaters on the ice (or violates the 5-ft substitution rule). (Rule 74.)

**Trapezoid.** The restricted area behind each net where the goalkeeper may play the puck — 6' wide at the goal line, 28' wide at the end boards. (Rule 1.8 / 27.8.)

**Tripping.** Placing the stick, knee, foot, arm, hand or elbow so as to cause an opponent to trip or fall. (Rule 57.)

**Unsportsmanlike Conduct.** Catch-all for non-physical misconduct directed at anyone other than an official. (Rule 75.)

**Video Goal Judge.** The off-ice official who, with the Situation Room, reviews potential goals and reviewable plays. (Rule 37.)

**Wash Out.** Hand signal (both arms swept sideways at shoulder level, palms down) used to wave off a goal, an icing, an off-side, a hand pass, or a high-sticking-the-puck violation. (Rule 29.35.)

**Zone.** Defending zone (own goal), Neutral zone (middle), Attacking zone (opponent's goal). (Rule 1.6.)

### "Is It a Penalty? Quick Decoder"

| Action | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Hooking the stick of a forechecker | Minor for hooking. |
| Hitting a guy from behind into the boards | Major + game misconduct (Rule 43). |
| Stick that comes up on a follow-through and grazes a face | Minor for high-sticking (4 min if blood/injury). |
| Punch in a scrum, both guys gloves down | Two majors for fighting; coincidental. |
| Shoving a player out of the front of the crease | Allowed unless defender plays the puck unfairly. |
| Goalie covers a puck with three opponents nearby | Allowed (he's actually being checked). |
| Goalie covers a puck with no opponent near | Minor for delay of game. |
| Skater clears the puck over the glass from his own zone | Minor for delay of game (puck over glass — challengeable). |
| Skater clears the puck over the glass from neutral zone | No penalty. |
| Attacking player kicks the puck and it goes in the net | No goal. |
| Attacking player's skate redirects the puck and it goes in | Goal. |
| High stick that touches the puck before it goes in | No goal if contact was above the crossbar; goal allowed if below. |
| Goalie pulled, defender throws his stick at the puck-carrier | Goal awarded. |
| Goalie plays the puck along the boards behind the net | Allowed only if inside the trapezoid; minor if outside. |

### Common Game-Situation Decoder

**You see a referee with his arm in the air.**
Delayed penalty. The offending team is about to commit an infraction; play continues until they touch the puck.

**The whistle blows but the referee says "no goal."**
Possible reasons (most common first): puck didn't fully cross the line; goalie interference (Rule 69); high stick above the crossbar (Rule 80); distinct kicking motion (Rule 49); puck off an official (Rule 78); attacker batted it in with hand (Rule 67).

**"Penalty kill" vs. "Power play."**
Same situation, two sides. The team short a player is on the penalty kill; the team with the man-advantage is on the power play.

**"Pulling the goalie."**
A team trailing late in the game (typically with under 2 minutes left) skates its goalie off the ice for a sixth attacker. The opposing team can score on the empty net to seal the win.

**"Hat trick."**
A player scores three goals in one game. Spectators throw hats onto the ice; this is one of the only times Rule 63.5 (objects on the ice) is not penalized.

**"4-on-4" / "3-on-3."**
Coincidental minors at the same stoppage. With no other penalties in effect, each team plays one short for 2 minutes. Regular-season overtime is always 3-on-3.

**Hybrid Icing in plain English.**
The puck is shot from behind your half of the red line, untouched, across the opposing goal line. The linesperson watches the foot race. If, by the time anyone reaches the end-zone face-off dot, the defender would have won the race, **whistle now, before the collision**. If the attacker would have won, no icing.

**Coach's Challenge in plain English.**
After a goal, a coach can challenge: (1) the attacking team was off-side, (2) some other stoppage was missed in the offensive zone before the goal, or (3) the goalie was interfered with. After a defensive-zone puck-over-glass minor, the defending coach can also challenge claiming the puck hit something before going out. Failed challenge = 2-minute delay-of-game (4 minutes if already failed once in the game).

**Why a fight is 5 minutes, not 2.**
Fighting is a major penalty (Rule 46.14). Both fighters get 5:00 — coincidentals, so the teams remain at full strength.

**Why the offensive team picks which face-off dot.**
After an icing, a delay-of-game minor, or any penalty creating a power play, the **non-offending team** picks the end-zone dot. After accidental net displacement, the attacking team picks.
